## Overview of Nonforfeiture Practices

"Nonforfeiture" means that the policyholder receives something when they surrender their policy or stop paying premiums
I.e. they don't "forfeit" everything

The main purpose of nonforfeiture options is to pay the policyholder their fair share of the equity in the policy

Nonforfeiture values can be based on 2 primary views:

1. Retrospective - the accumulation of policyholder equity since policy issue based on actual experience
2. Prospective - the value of future benefits based on anticipated experience

- Persister approach - assumes policies only terminate from mortality or expiry
- Whole contract approach - reflects any policyholder option to terminate/convert the contract

These two views can be quite different depending on experience

## Page 4
For example, if mortality is trending higher, the PV of future benefits will be high, causing the prospective value to be higher than the retrospective value

More on these views in the sections that follow

# Definitions 

Fund - retrospective accumulation of credits and debits
Cash surrender value (CSV) - fund value less surrender charges
Surrender charges - amounts intended to cover the company's acquisition expenses

## Types of Nonforfeiture Options

These options are mentioned and sprinkled throughout the study note, but I've consolidated the main points here.

## 1. Cash Surrender

Policyholder receives the CSV of the policy

## 2. Extended Term Insurance (ETI)

Full policy face is kept in force as long as CSV can support it
CSV declines as the cost of insurance is deducted
If CSV falls to zero, the policy will terminate
Advantages of ETI:

- Maximizes policyholder's insurance protection
- ETI expenses lower since coverage period is less than RPU
- Making ETI the automatic (default) option can reduce antiselection

Disadvantage of ETI: higher antiselection than other options
If you know you're going to die soon, ETI is a great option since it maximizes your death benefit and allows you to stop paying premiums immediately

## 3. Reduced Paid-Up (RPU)

Policy face amount is reduced to the point where it is paid-up with the current cash value

$$
\mathrm{CSV}_{x}=(\text { RPU Face }) A_{x}
$$

Policy stays in force until maturity at the lower face amount (like a smaller WL policy)
Advantages of RPU:

- Keeps original form and expiry date intact
- Low antiselection

## Page 5
# 4. Automatic Premium Loan (APL) 

Premiums are paid by taking out loans against the policy's CSV
Each successive premium increases the outstanding policy loan balance
Advantages of APL:

- The face amount of the policy remains the same
- The policy stays in force as long as CSV $>$ Loan Balance
- Policyholder can repay the loan at anytime and/or resume making premium payments
- Special benefits and riders remain in force

Disadvantages of APL

- Policy loan activity will increases
- Provides incentive to stop paying premiums
- Policyholder loan interest may be high
- Death protection may be reduced by loan balance
- Cannot be exercised if no CSV


## 5. Annuitization

Deferred annuity contracts may let the policyholder annuitize the cash value

## 6. Flexible UL Premium Payments

A flexible premium UL policy does not require any premium payments as long as CSV $>0$
A flexible premium UL policy where the policyholder isn't paying any premiums is functionally like ETI

## Methods for Calculating Nonforfeiture Options

## 1. Retrospective Method

Accumulation of credits and debits:
Fund $_{t}=$ Fund $_{t-1}+$ Premiums $_{t}+$ Interest $_{t}-$ Benefit Charges $_{t}-$ Expenses $_{t}-$ Profit $_{t}$
Currently used for variable annuities in the U.S.
This approach is easy for policyholders to understand but can limit product design
"No lapse guarantee" products guarantee a positive fund value even if CSV $<0$
Found on some UL products where cash value is low relative to surrender charges in the early years

Prospective and retrospective methods may give very different results for no-lapse designs

## Page 6
# 2. Prospective Method 

Forward-looking view including all significant benefits, appropriate share of company expenses, and a reasonable contribution to profits:

PV Future Benefits + PV Expenses + PV Profits - PV Premiums
Assumptions may be specified by regulators

## Disadvantages/Challenges:

- Harder to explain to policyholders than the retrospective method

Especially if bad experience leads to lower values

- If policy classes are broadly defined, experience will not be homogenous within the class (i.e. will vary by gender and risk factors)
- Flexible premium products (UL) require an assumption about future premium payments
- Premium payments affect death benefits for certain products

Does a better job of maintaining parity between persisting and terminating policyholders since current values reflect expected future experience

## 3. Net Premium Method

Same as the prospective method, except use a level net premium calculated at issue:
PV Level Net Premiums $=$ PV Future Benefits + PV Acquisition Costs

The net premium is a constant percentage of gross premiums
This method is currently required in the U.S. for traditional policies
Regulators prescribe assumptions for mortality and interest as well as max level of acquisition expense

Like the prospective method, the actuary needs to make some kind of assumption about future premiums for flexible premium UL

May assume some percentage of premiums are paid ( $0-100 \%$ )

## 4. Single Premium Method

Assumes the policyholder "borrows" a single premium to purchase the policy
As the policyholder makes premium payments, the loan is reduced, and the policyholder gains equity

Very different from other approaches

## Nonforfeiture Practices in the U.S.

Net Premium Method required for traditional life
Retrospective required for UL

## Page 7
Required nonforfeiture values date back to the 19th century
Nonforfeiture options try to balance 2 socially desirable but competing objectives:

1. Keep cost of insurance as low as possible
2. Pay out policyholder's equity on surrender

# Different Views on Equity in the U.S. 

- Policyholder view - equitable sum of gross premiums with interest less a reasonable charge for cost of protection
- Company view - compensation for costs and reasonable profit for taking risk
- Agent view - reasonable reward for consummating a sale
- Regulator view - amount that maximizes policyholder's return but doesn't threaten the company's solvency

Other Factors Affecting Equity

- Who pays cost of early terminations? (remaining policyholders, company, agent)
- Expense allocation method

## Standard Nonforfeiture Law (SNL) for Life Insurance

It's worth emphasizing the SNL is an entirely different law than SVL. SNL prescribes minimum nonforfeiture values. However, SNL does reference the SVL and VM for the interest rate assumption.

SNL defines the minimum guaranteed cash surrender value and reduced paid-up insurance that an insurer can offer

## Minimum Cash Surrender Value

$$
\operatorname{MinCV}_{t}=\max \left[\mathrm{PVFB}_{t}-\mathrm{PVAdjPrem}_{t}, 0\right]
$$

## Page 10
where:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { AdjPrem } & =\text { adjusted premium for the first policy year } \\
& =\frac{\mathrm{EA}_{x}+\mathrm{PVFB}_{0}}{\bar{a}_{x}} \\
\mathrm{EA}_{x} & =0.01 \times \text { Face }+1.25 \times \min [0.04 \times \text { Face, NFNLP }] \\
\text { NFNLP } & =\text { nonforfeiture net level premium } \\
& =\frac{\mathrm{PVFB}_{0}}{\bar{a}_{x}}
\end{aligned}
$$

For a level face policy, just use the actual level face amount, but if the face amount is non-level, then use the average face amount over the first 10 years only

$$
\text { Face }=\frac{1}{10} \sum_{t=0}^{9} \text { Face }_{t}
$$

If you were perceptive, you noticed that the adjusted premium in the book is defined as "adjusted premium for the first policy year." This subtlety was probably included to generalize the formula for non-level premiums even though the authors never mention non-level GPs in this chapter. Just be aware that in reality, the SNL defines adjusted premiums as a level $\%$ of GPs, which means a more general approach would be:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { AdjPrem } \% & =\frac{\mathrm{EA}_{x}+\mathrm{PVFB}_{0}}{\mathrm{PVGP}_{0}} \\
\mathrm{PVAdjPrem}_{t} & =\text { AdjPrem } \% \times \mathrm{PVGP}_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

# Policyholder Dividends 

Life insurance policies that pay policyholder dividends are called participating policies ${ }^{2}$
Most participating policies are sold by mutual life insurance companies, which are insurance companies owned by their policyholders

- Contrast with a stock life insurance company, which is owned by shareholders ${ }^{3}$


## Dividend options offered to participating policyholders:

1. Receive the dividend in cash
2. Apply dividend to some or all of the next premium due
3. Deposit the dividend with the insurer to earn interest
4. Purchase paid-up additional (PUA) insurance coverage without evidence of insurability
5. Purchase one-year term (OYT) insurance without evidence of insurability

No. 4 is the most common

## Dividend Scale

Dividend scale - set of rates that details how divisible surplus will be distributed participating policies

Divisible surplus - aggregate amount of capital to be distributed as policy dividends
Division of surplus is at the sole discretion of the board, but they have a duty to be "fair and equitable"

The board is advised by a senior dividend actuary with extensive experience in determining policyholder dividends

## Duties of the dividend actuary when developing the dividend scale:

1. Review experience of the each dividend class: expense, policy loan, mortality and policy termination
2. Determine investment earnings of the assets supporting the participating policies: interest, dividends, capital gains/losses

This is essentially a complicated "return of premium" mechanism because the surplus is generated by excess premiums that were not needed to pay for mortality, expenses, etc. This also means that participating policies will tend to charge higher premiums than non-participating.
    ${ }^{3}$ Stock life insurance companies can still issue participating life insurance within specific participating blocks of the company.

3. Determine amount of earnings from other lines of business that will be reflected in the dividend scale
4. Develop dividend factors for allocating divisible surplus to individual participating policies
5. Perform various analyses to confirm compliance with applicable law, ASOPs, and the insurer's dividend policy

The board strives to strike a balance between:

1. Withholding enough surplus to absorb:

- Unexpected future losses
- Fund future growth of the company

2. Distributing surplus to its owners (the participating policyholders who contributed to the surplus)

# Contribution Principle 

ASOP 15 requires the dividend actuary to use the contribution principle for determining dividends unless a different basis is preferable, reasonable, and appropriate based on the actuary's judgment

- Must be applied annually or over an extended period of time

Fundamental concept underlying the contribution principle: allocate divisible surplus to policies proportionally based on each policy's contribution

3-factor dividend formula - common formula for determining policyholder dividends

- Assumptions for expense, mortality, and interest are set at policy issue, and dividends arise based on 3 components ("factors"):

1. Expense factor - difference between assumed and actual expenses
2. Mortality factor - difference between assumed and actual mortality cost
3. Interest factor - difference between actual and assumed investment earnings

- Mortality and interest are usually the largest factors overall
- Mortality is the largest in early years
- The interest factor can become significant in later policy years when accumulated assets are much higher

## Page 13
- Factors are generally based on the experience of a group of policies but also vary based on age, gender, and policy year

## Standard Nonforfeiture Law for Annuities

These sections cover the SNL for annuities: minimum guaranteed values that the insurer must provide (paid-up annuity benefits, CSVs, and DBs)

The value of any paid-up annuity benefit or CSV offered by the contract must be no less than the SNL minimum nonforfeiture value:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Min NF Amount }_{t}= & \left(\text { Min NF Amount }_{t-1}+(87.5 \%)\left(\text { Consideration }_{t}\right)-50-\text { PremTax }_{t}\right)(1+i) \\
& -\text { PartialWd }_{t}-\text { PolicyLoan }_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

$i=$ minimum SNL Interest Rate $=5$-Year CMT $-1.25 \%$

- 5-Year CMT $=5$-year Constant Maturity Treasury reported by the Federal Reserve rounded to the nearest $0.05 \%$
- $i$ is capped at $3 \%$ and floored at $1 \%$
- Can subtract an additional 1\% for indexed annuities (EIAs)

$$
i=5 \text {-Year CMT }-2.25 \%
$$

- The $1 \%$ floor still applies

The minimum nonforfeiture amount above essentially regulates the amount of surrender charges the insurer can apply on surrender. As with UL, the CSV of the DA contract at any point in time is the current FV - surrender charges. This CSV can't be less than the minimum nonforfeiture amount above.

## Settlement Options

Settlement option - the option to convert the accumulated value into some type of payout annuity

Most deferred annuities offer these common settlement options (i.e. types of payout or immediate annuities):

1. Annuity certain - makes periodic payments for a specified period of time even if the annuitant dies (not life contingent)

## Page 14
2. Straight-life annuity - makes periodic payments only while the annuitant is alive (life contingent)
3. Life annuity with a period certain - makes periodic payments for life but guarantees payments for a certain period (even if annuitant dies before end of certain period)
4. Refund annuity - a life annuity that makes additional payments on or after death

- Cash refund annuity - pays a lump sum payment $=\max (0$, Purchase Price $-\sum$ Annuity Payments Made Through Death)
- Installment refund annuity - continues making payments to a beneficiary after the annuitant's death until sum of all payments = purchase price

5. Joint and survivorship annuity - a life annuity on two annuitants that continues making payments to the survivor after one person dies (payments stop completely on 2nd death)

- Payment is usually reduced after first death however (e.g. $70 \%$ of original)

If the annuitant dies during the accumulation phase (i.e. before electing one of the above settlement options), the contract must provide a $\mathrm{DB} \geq$ minimum nonforfeiture value at the time of death

- Common practice is to waive any surrender charges and pay the full FV on death

## Background and History of NGEs

## Principal characteristics of NGEs:

1. Affect the cost of contractual benefits and policy values
2. Not guaranteed except for minimum or maximum values
3. May be changed unilaterally by the insurer, subject to contractual/legal restrictions

Common NGEs: COI charges, expense loads, credited interest rates, index parameters, nonguaranteed bonuses, and indeterminate premiums

## NGEs do not include policyholder dividends ${ }^{4}$

- Policyholder dividends are paid on participating policies
- Policyholder dividends = par policyholders' share of a par block's profits

NGEs are mainly based on expectations of future experience, while dividends are based primarily on actual past experience

NGEs arose in response to problems and opportunities in the 1970s-1980s:

- Traditional guaranteed life/annuity product design lacked flexibility to adapt to rapidly changing conditions
- Interest rates and inflation were very high and volatile
- Mortality improved substantially, but pricing actuaries didn't know if it would continue
- Insurers were reluctant to price possibly temporary favorable experience changes into long-term guarantees
- Non-insurance accumulation products began offering higher credited rates
- Increased computer power made it possible to develop more sophisticated products

Initially, the partial elimination of guarantees resulted in lower pricing

# Most significant early NGE product designs: 

- Fixed deferred annuities with discretionary crediting rates, subject to guaranteed minimums
- UL with crediting rates, bonus elements, and charges, subject to contractual restraints
- Was said to "unbundle" the death protection and investment aspects of WL
- Early designs had unattractive expense charge structures but quickly changed to be more competitive
- Indeterminate premium plans with rates that could be changed after issue, subject to maximums

FDAs and UL products gave policyholders more flexibility over contract funding, and provided insurers more flexibility in setting credits/charges

- Changes that were not allowed by the contract
- Changes that calculated inappropriately
- Changes that recoup past losses

- Future changes to NGEs result from variances from expected values

# NGE Types, Determination, and Revision 

## Types of NGEs

- Credited rates that are adjustable based on investment performance subject to guaranteed minimums
- Premium loads and other expenses subject to guaranteed maximums
- COI charges - vary by underwriting class, sex, attained age, issue age, and policy year
- Subject to guaranteed max rates set to comply with SNL and tax law
- Guaranteed rates may be higher if anticipated mortality $>$ nonforfeiture mortality
- Index parameters on FIA and IUL - cap rates and participation rates subject to guaranteed rates specified in the policy
- Nonguaranteed bonuses - credits to the AV or refund of charges under certain conditions
- VA rider charges for enhanced guaranteed living benefit riders, subject to max values specified in the policy
- Indeterminate premiums - traditional products with guaranteed and nonguaranteed premium scales


## Original NGE Determination

The pricing actuary must consider a product's purpose, market, buyer preferences, etc.
Considerations when balancing the insurer's profitability targets with consumer needs:

- Expected mortality
- Depends on underwriting, target market, risk classes, policy sizes, etc.
- COI charges relative to expected mortality
- Policy load and expense charges
- How to cover policy issue expenses
- Portion of the premium used to cover these costs
- Source of revenue for flexible premium products
- Basis for credited interest
- Whether investment yields will be sufficient to cover credited interest and other costs
- Use of new money vs. portfolio method for setting credited rates

## Page 18
Experience studies and sensitivity testing are important when pricing products with NGEs
Pricing is an iterative/holistic process

- As assumptions are modified, assess profitability/competitiveness individually and in aggregate


# Impacts of Revising NGEs 

Products with NGEs have lower premiums than fully guaranteed products, all else equal

- NGE policyholders bear more risk of lower benefits (or higher future premiums) if experience deteriorates

Insurers can provide illustrations of possible future values to policyholders
NGE revisions are allowed if experience doesn't align with anticipated experience factors (AEFs) set in the original pricing

# ASOP 2 Determination Process for Revising NGE Scales on Inforce Policies 

1. Review any prior determinations, including the original at product pricing

- If not available, use a reasonable approach to reconstruct
- Consider whether NGE scales are consistent with policy language

2. Analyze how experience is emerging relative to what was anticipated at the prior determination

- Remember that the expectation is not to revise unless anticipated experience factors change

3. Consider whether to recommend a revision to NGE scales

- Not purely a mathematical exercise (consider multiple criteria)
- Other stakeholders involved/affected: senior management, marketing, legal, admin teams

4. If doing a revision, determine the revised NGE scales

- Should be purely prospective: focus on future profitability levels
- Objective should NOT be to recoup past losses or distribute past gains

## Pre-AG 49

In 1995, the NAIC created the Life Insurance Illustrations Model Regulation ("Reg \#582") ${ }^{24}$

- Created illustration requirements for existing life insurance products (in general)
- Did not address product-specific illustration requirements


## IUL was created in 1997

- No official guidance on illustrating index-based accounts
- Insurers created inconsistent IUL illustrations


## 2015: AG 49 Created

By 2015, IUL was offered by an increasing number of carriers
Inconsistent illustrations resulted from lack of guidance and over-reliance on actuarial judgment ${ }^{25}$

## AG 49 was adopted to increase IUL illustration consistency

- Defined a max illustrated index credited rate based on a back-casting formula using the BIA

[^0]:    ${ }^{24}$ Other syllabus readings may refer to this as "Model 582"
    ${ }^{25}$ Even though there is some uncertainty in the future credited rate of a regular UL policy, there is much more uncertainty in an IUL policy's future index credits, which are affected by index performance and adjustable index parameters like caps, participation rates, etc.

## Page 37
- BIA $=$ Benchmark Index Account $^{26}$
- 1-year PTP index account based on the S\&P 500
- $100 \%$ participation and $0 \%$ floor
- Limited participating policy loan spread to 100 bps
- Limits assumed earned rate to $145 \%$ of net investment income rate
- Outlines additional required disclosures


# 2020: AG 49-A 

After 2015, insurers found ways to create more "appealing" illustrations

- Insurers began offering cap buy-ups and index multipliers in exchange for an account value charge (e.g. $5 \%)^{27}$
- Leverage $=$ index credits in excess of hedge cost
- BIA Illustrated Rate $=$ Option Budget + Leverage
- Multiplier Design Illustrated Rate $=$ Option Budget + Leverage $\times$ Multiplier
- Products became more complex; illustrations remained inconsistent

AG 49-A was created to mandate that multipliers or other enhancements cannot result in better illustrations

- I.e must assume multiplier $=1.0$ in the illustrated rate formula
- Max Illustrated Rate $=$ BIA Illustrated Rate
- Also reduced the spread on participating loans to $50 \mathrm{bps}^{28}$


## 2023: AG 49-A Quick-Fix

Even after AG 49-A, insurers continued finding ways to outperform the BIA
Volatility control funds allowed for lower hedge costs and fixed bonuses

- Volatility-controlled indexes have lower volatility than the S\&P 500 assumed by the BIA (cheaper to hedge) ${ }^{29}$

[^0]
[^0]:    ${ }^{26}$ The source reading omits an important detail about the BIA: it also assumes the same cap as the actual product.
    ${ }^{27}$ The source reading doesn't fully explain how these features work, but they allowed the insurer to offer higher caps and/or credit bonus interest on top of the normal index credit for a fee. It was simply a way of gaming AG 49 to show a higher illustrated credited rate to make IUL policies more attractive to prospective buyers.
    ${ }^{28}$ The source reading doesn't explain the point of this, but it was done to limit how much loan leverage insurers could illustrate (i.e. situations where the policy loan rate < illustrated credits). This is a different "leverage" concept than the leverage related to index credits in excess of hedge costs.
    ${ }^{29}$ The BIA uses the S\&P 500 regardless, but AG 49 allowed the insurer to reflect the actual index if different from the S\&P 500, so this move exploited that loophole.

- Hedge savings are passed to IUL policyholders through fixed bonuses, which enhance illustrations
- Vol-Control Illustrated Rate $=$ Option Budget + Leverage + Fixed Bonus
- Products became even more complex and confusing to consumers

AG 49-A "Quick-Fix" (aka "AG 49-B"): max illustrated option profit can't exceed BIA profit

- Max Illustrated Credit $=$ Option Budget $\times \frac{\text { BIA Illustrated Rate }}{\text { BIA Option Budget }}+$ Fixed Bonus

# How Dividends Work 

## Definitions of dividend:

- Return of premium that was not needed
- Distribution of surplus that the insurer does not need to retain any longer

In the US and Canada, policyholder dividends are usually based on the contribution principle

- Each policy's dividend $=$ allocation of aggregate divisible in proportion to what the policy contributed to surplus based on experience factor classes


## Process To Set Dividends

- Declaration by Board, after recommendation by management and/or dividend actuary
- Board determines the amount of divisible surplus
* Must balance the need to pay higher dividends (competitiveness) with the need to retain surplus for future needs (new business, solvency)
* Consider surplus impact on ratings
- Board authorizes a dividend scale recommended by management and/or a dividend actuary
* Interactive, back and forth process
* Usually starts with prior year and looks at changes in experience since then
- Distribution to individual policies according to dividend scale adopted
- Dividends are usually paid annually based on the dividend scale in effect on a policy's anniversary
- Ideally the dividend scale will be known at least a month before each policy anniversary so the dividend amount can be communicated on the policyholder's premium notice
- Off-anniversary dividends are payable on death and sometimes other terminations (beyond scope of paper)

## 3 principal sources of earnings for dividend payments:

1. Mortality - biggest source initially and rises gradually, then falls as net amount at risk declines
2. Investment - small initially but eventually becomes the dominant source later as the policy accumulates more assets
3. Expense - fairly small and constant over the life of the policy

## Distributable Earnings

Distributable earnings are equal to solvency earnings minus the increase in required capital
"Solvency earnings" is a generic phrase for US statutory earnings. All of the mechanics here are based on US statutory accounting concepts. In Canada, there is a single set of books for both solvency and GAAP reserves (now mostly based on IFRS 17). So just think "US statutory" when you see "solvency" in this chapter.

Positive distributable earnings represent the maximum amount that can be paid to stockholders in a given period

Negative distributable earnings represent the amount stockholders need to pay in to keep the company going

## Pre-Tax Earnings

$$
\begin{aligned}

& -\operatorname{DACAmort}(t)+\operatorname{InvIncRC}(t)
\end{aligned}
$$

The net GAAP reserve is also called the earnings reserve in this chapter:

$$
\operatorname{EarnRes}(t)=\operatorname{BenRes}(t)-\operatorname{DAC}(t)
$$

# Taxable Earnings 

Calculating taxable earnings usually requires making two types of adjustments to solvency earnings:

1. Timing differences - differences in earnings bases that will eventually reverse over time (a.k.a. "temporary differences")

- Examples of timing differences between solvency and taxable earnings:
- Reserve calculations - interest, mortality, methods
- Treatment of assets - gains/losses, carrying values, amortization
- Different treatment of liabilities for incurred claims, policyholder dividends, etc.
- Liabilities not recognized for tax purposes - reserves to stabilize investment returns (IMR), reserves to offset future capital losses, required capital
- This chapter only focuses on timing differences between reserve bases

$$
\operatorname{TimingDiff}(t)=\operatorname{SolvResIncr}(t)-\operatorname{TaxResIncr}(t)
$$

- If the only differences in solvency earnings and taxable earnings is the reserve basis, total earnings will be the same in both cases (only the timing is affected)
- Regulators prefer more conservative values for assets and liabilities (tends to lower solvency earnings)
- Tax authorities prefer higher tax revenue (higher asset values and lower liability values)
- Significant timing differences should be reflected in pricing

2. Permanent differences - differences that will not reverse

- Examples:
- Nontaxable investment income
- Tax credits for research and development
- Non-deductible expenses
- This chapter will only consider permanent differences based on non-deductible investment income

$$
\operatorname{PermDiff}(t)=-\operatorname{InvIncome}(t) \times \operatorname{NonTaxInvPct}(t)
$$

# Capital Gains and Losses 

Capital gains/losses can create both timing differences and permanent differences

- Example: in the US, capital gains are immediately taxable, but capital losses can only offset capital gains

Capital losses can be carried forward up to 3 years and used against capital gains

- However if not used, they expire, creating a permanent difference

For example, suppose the company realizes capital gains of 40 and capital losses of 100 during 2020. This will result in zero taxable capital gains 2020. The company can carry forward the remaining 60 of capital losses to offset any capital gains in 2021-2023. However, if there are no more capital gains in those 3 years, solvency earnings will be permanently lower than taxable earnings by 60 (because the remaining 60 of capital losses never affected taxable earnings).

## Formulas for Taxable Earnings

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { TaxableEarn }(t) & =\operatorname{PreTaxSolvEarn}(t)+\text { TimingDiff }(t)+\operatorname{PermDiff}(t) \\
& =\operatorname{PreTaxSolvEarn}(t)+\operatorname{SolvResIncr}(t)-\operatorname{TaxResIncr}(t)+\operatorname{PermDiff}(t) \\
& =\operatorname{ProdCashFlow}(t)+\operatorname{InvIncome}(t)-\operatorname{TaxResIncr}(t)+\operatorname{PermDiff}(t)
\end{aligned}
$$

When calculating taxes, use the appropriate tax rate (or rates if the rates are progressive)
Assuming there is only one tax rate:

$$
\operatorname{Tax}(t)=\operatorname{TaxRate} \times \operatorname{TaxableEarn}(t)
$$

## Tax Losses

A company cannot pay negative taxes in total for any given tax year
Since life insurance policies usually generate losses in the first year, the company will want to use those taxable losses to offset other taxable gains

- Generally not a concern for large insurers, but could be an issue for a small company that doesn't have enough positive taxable earnings from other sources

Insurers who cannot immediately deduct tax losses when selling a new life insurance product will require more capital investment

- Results in a lower ROI, etc.


## Tax on Investment Income Less Expenses (I-E)

This section of the chapter describes a fairly archaic form of life insurance taxation that is an alternative to taxing earnings (as described in the previous section). The "I-E" method is not used in the US or Canada, and there has been a global movement away from it for quite some time. Therefore, this material is probably less relevant to the exam.

## Page 67
The tax on $I-E$ attempts to indirectly tax the annual increase in the policyholders' value in addition to the insurer's earnings

$$
\operatorname{InvIncome}(t)-\operatorname{Exp}(t)=\underbrace{\operatorname{Ben}(t)+\operatorname{SolvResIncr}(t)-\operatorname{Prem}(t)}_{\text {value created for policyholders }}+\operatorname{PreTaxSolvEarn}(t)
$$

The solvency reserve increase can be thought of as future policyholder benefits (like a cash value)

- So it's like taxing policyholders on the value they received in excess of premiums paid in In the simplest case, the tax on $I-E$ is simply:

$$
\text { IETaxRate } \times[\operatorname{InvIncome}(t)-\operatorname{Exp}(t)]
$$

## Deferred Taxes

For GAAP accounting purposes, it is undesirable for taxes to be a non-level percentage of earnings

Instead of reporting the actual amount of taxes paid, GAAP accounting will report taxes on an

## Page 68
accrued basis so that taxes emerge as a level $\%$ of stockholder earnings

$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{Tax}(t) & =\text { TaxRate } \times \text { TaxableEarnings } \\
\text { AccruedTax }(t) & =\text { TaxRate } \times \text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t) \\
\operatorname{DefTaxProv}(t) & =\text { AccruedTax }(t)-\operatorname{Tax}(t) \\
\text { AfterTaxStockEarn }(t) & =\text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t)-\text { AccruedTax }(t) \\
& =(1-\text { TaxRate }) \times \text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t) \\
\operatorname{DefTaxLiab}(t) & =\operatorname{DefTaxLiab}(t-1)+\operatorname{DefTaxProv}(t) \\
& =\text { TaxRate } \times[\operatorname{TaxRes}(t)-\operatorname{EarnRes}(t)]
\end{aligned}
$$

The deferred tax liability (or asset if negative) is the corresponding GAAP balance sheet item

- DefTaxLiab $>0$ when the current tax reserve is greater than the net GAAP reserves (earnings reserves)
- This is the most common situation
- Indicates that taxable earnings have been lower than GAAP earnings to date (because the tax reserve built up faster)
- Eventually the timing difference between tax and GAAP reserves will reverse, causing future taxes to increase
- If DefTaxLiab $<0$, the DefTaxLiab is actually a deferred tax asset (exact same math though)
- I.e. taxes will be lower in the future relative to the past

The DefTaxLiab has mystified many exam candidates before you and will likely mystify many in the future. However, it is simply an interest-free reserve for future taxes under GAAP accounting. If you're familiar with the basic idea of GAAP accounting, you'll know that there are many GAAP mechanisms designed to levelize revenues and costs so that earnings are more stable over time. This is based on that same goal. Note that you can also write after-tax stockholder earnings as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { AfterTaxStockEarn }(t) & =\text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t)-\operatorname{Tax}(t)-\operatorname{DefTaxLiabIncr}(t) \\
& =\operatorname{PreTaxStockEarn}(t)-\operatorname{Tax}(t)-\operatorname{DefTaxProv}(t) \\
& =\operatorname{PreTaxStockEarn}(t)-\operatorname{Tax}(t)-[\operatorname{AccruedTax}(t)-\operatorname{Tax}(t)] \\
& =\operatorname{PreTaxStockEarn}(t)-\operatorname{AccruedTax}(t)
\end{aligned}
$$

In other words, the deferred tax liability serves to "convert" actual taxes into taxes reported on an accrued basis. This forces taxes to emerge as a level \% (the tax rate itself) of pre-tax stockholder earnings

$$
\frac{\text { AccruedTax }(t)}{\text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t)}=\frac{\text { TaxRate } \times \text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t)}{\text { PreTaxStockEarn }(t)}=\text { TaxRate }
$$

# Appendix: DAC Tax 

Common point of confusion: Many people wrongly assume this is a GAAP accounting concept because of the word "DAC." DAC tax has nothing to do with GAAP accounting. DAC tax is specific to US tax

## Page 69
accounting and affects the amount of taxable earnings used to calculate the tax on earnings. It borrows its name from the GAAP version of "DAC" because somewhat like GAAP DAC, DAC tax capitalizes and defers amounts into the future.

DAC tax is a US tax accounting mechanism that serves to temporarily increase taxable income as premiums are collected and is then reversed out of taxable income over a period of years in the future

The net effect is to accelerate tax payments to the IRS because there is no interest adjustment
The amount added to taxable income each period is expressed as a $\%$ of premium collected ( $7.7 \%$ for individual life and $1.75 \%$ for annuities)

- These amounts are then subtracted from taxable income on a prorated basis over the next 10 years

For a given year, DACTaxAmount $(t)=$ additions to DAC tax - deductions from DAC tax
For example, for each premium collected (in any policy year) for a life insurance product:

| Year | Add to Taxable Earnings | Deduct From Taxable Earnings |
| :--: | :--: | :--: |
| 1 | $7.7 \%$ | $5 \%$ of $7.7 \%$ |
| $2-10$ | 0 | $10 \%$ of $7.7 \%$ |
| 11 | 0 | $5 \%$ of $7.7 \%$ |

This amount is added to the taxable earnings calculated earlier:

$$
\text { TaxableEarn }(t)=\text { PreTaxSolvEarn }(t)+\text { TimingDiff }(t)+\text { PermDiff }(t)+\text { DACTaxAmount }(t)
$$

Rather than memorize the DACTaxAmount formula shown in the book, I think it's far easier to just understand conceptually how to do the math. See the video lesson for straightforward example that you can easily generalize for any situation and also more information on how DACTax impacts the profitability of life insurance products.

## High-Level Summary of VM-20 Requirements

The principles-based methodology in VM-20 requires the calculation of 3 reserves:

## 1. Net premium reserve (NPR)

- Seriatim formulaic reserve using prescribed assumptions for mortality, lapse, and interest
- The valuation interest rate is locked in at issue, but prescribed mortality and lapses could change after issue


## 2. Deterministic reserve (DR)

- Aggregate gross premium reserve $=$ PV of pretax liability cash flows at discount rates under a prescribed scenario
- Discount rate based on existing assets and reinvestments under VM-20 assumptions
- Experience assumptions = mix of company experience and prescribed
- Includes margins on mortality, etc.
- After issue, the DR will be affected by:

## Page 72
- Emerging experience (mortality, lapse, expense, policyholder behavior, etc.)
- Changing asset earnings
- Changes in CSO valuation mortality
- Revisions to mortality margins
- Changing treasury rates
- Changes in reinsurance


# 3. Stochastic reserve (SR) 

- Aggregate CTE 70 greatest present value of accumulated deficiencies over a range of stochastic scenarios
- Liability cash flows based on same assumptions as DR
- All of the post-issue impacts for DR also apply
- Asset modeling is critical $\Rightarrow$ variations in assets across scenarios drives the reserve
- SR discount rate varies by scenario: $1.05 \times 1$-year treasury scenario rates


## VM-20 Reserve $=$ NPR + Excess Reserve $\pm$ Premiums Due or Deferred

- Excess Reserve $=\max (D R, S R)-N P R$
- Premiums Due or Deferred $=$ technical accounting adjustment based on the timing of policyholder premiums (not important for LP)

Presentation note: It is important to always express the final reserve in terms of the NPR + the excess as shown above (e.g. don't just report the total $S R$ if $S R$ is the highest)

# Measuring Profitability with Market-Consistent Embedded Value (MCEV) 

Assets are held at a trading value, while non-traded liabilities are valued using replicating portfolios

MCEV $=$ Value of In-Force (VIF) + Required Capital + Free Surplus
VIF $=$ PV Future Profits - Value of Options and Guarantees - Cost of Non-Hedgeable Risks Frictional Cost of Required Capital

Required Capital $=\mathrm{MV}$ capital allocated to support business (e.g. rating target)
Free Surplus $=\mathrm{MV}$ any extra allocated capital not needed to support business

## Page 84

MCEV has asymmetric interest rate risk because the cost of guarantees rises sharply when interest rates rate fall

- -100 bps interest rate change $=-9 \%$ change in MCEV
- +100 bps interest rate change $=+3 \%$ change in MCEV

Mortality and morbidity risks are more symmetric with plus/minus shocks, so natural hedges are possible

Earnings analyses are useful for showing how MCEV changes period to period by component:

1. MCEV of new business added
2. Unwinding of MCEV on existing business

- Planned roll-forward of discounting
- Interest on required capital and surplus
- Expected investment income
- Expected release of non-hedgeable costs

3. Experience (past) variances and assumption (forward-looking) updates
4. Economic variances

- Changes in economic variable beyond the company's control (e.g. interest rates)

# Calculating the Economic Value of Liabilities (EVL) 

1. Project liability cash flows (premiums, claims, expenses, etc.)
2. Project frictional costs
3. Calculate net liability cash flow at each future point in time

Net $\mathrm{CF}=$ Premiums - Claims - Expenses - Frictional Costs
4. $\mathrm{EVL}=\mathrm{PV}(\mathrm{Net} \mathrm{CF})$ at risk-free rate

The EVL is based on risk-free rates because the cash flows can be replicated with risk-free assets
Buy zero-coupon bonds to match years with negative net CF
Borrow (short) zero-coupon bonds to match years with positive net CF
EVL = Market Value of Long Bonds - Market Value of Short Bonds
EVL Example

| Year | Premiums | Claims | Expenses | Frictional Costs | Net CF |
| :--: | :--: | :--: | :--: | :--: | :--: |
| 0 | 100 |  | 87 | 0 | 13 |
| 1 | 100 | 50 | 5 | 2 | 43 |
| 2 |  | 50 | 5 | 2 | -57 |

The company will receive 43 in year 1 and will have to pay out 57 in year 2
Build a replicating portfolio:
Buy zero-coupon bonds maturing for 57 in 2 years
Short zero-coupon bonds maturing for 43 in year 1
The long 2-year bonds will fund the net liability payment in year 2, and the borrowed 1-year bonds will be repaid with the positive cash flow in year 1

## Page 92
Assume the risk-free rate is $3 \%$ all years:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{EVL}_{0} & =-\frac{43}{1.03}+\frac{57}{1.03^{2}} \\
& =-41.75+53.73 \\
& =11.98
\end{aligned}
$$

This is the net market value of the risk-free replicating portfolio at time 0

# Economic Profit and IRR 

## Economic Profit at Issue

Economic Profit $=$ Net CF - Change in EVL
From the previous example:

$$
\text { Economic Profit at Issue }=13-11.98=1.02
$$

In other words, the company collected 13 upfront but only had to spend 11.98 to set up the replicating portfolio

The IRR of the cash flows is:

$$
\begin{aligned}
13 & =-\frac{43}{1+\mathrm{IRR}}+\frac{57}{(1+\mathrm{IRR})^{2}} \\
\mathrm{IRR} & =1.44 \%
\end{aligned}
$$

The IRR is the company's cost of borrowing from the policyholder along with the cost of other expenses and frictional costs

Since the company borrowed at a rate of $1.44 \%$, then invested in assets yielding $3 \%$, the company earned a spread of $1.56 \%$

This illustrates the 2nd way that insurers differ from leveraged investment funds: the borrowing advantage

IRR is a fickle animal, especially if the cash flow signs switch a lot. As such, the authors recommend focusing on the absolute economic profit. However, in simple cases like the one above, you might be asked to calculate it on the exam.

## Economic Profit After Issue

At issue, expected economic profit in future periods is zero because the change in EVL is exactly offset by the cash flows and investment return

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { Economic Profit }_{t}= & \text { Net CF After Frictional Costs }_{t} \\
& +\operatorname{InvReturn}_{t}-\left(\mathrm{EVL}_{t}-\mathrm{EVL}_{t-1}\right) \\
\operatorname{InvReturn}_{t}= & r \times \mathrm{EVL}_{t-1}
\end{aligned}
$$

## Page 93
This is really the same formula used above at issue; it's just that there's no InvReturn at issue.
Continuing the example, now one year after issue:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{EVL}_{1} & =\frac{57}{1.03}=55.34 \\
\operatorname{InvReturn}_{1} & =0.03 \times 11.98=0.36 \\
\Delta \mathrm{EVL}_{1} & =55.34-11.98=43.36 \\
\text { Economic Profit }_{1} & =\underbrace{43}_{\text {Net CF }}+\underbrace{0.36}_{\text {InvReturn }}-\underbrace{43.36}_{\Delta \mathrm{EVL}}=0
\end{aligned}
$$

Since the replicating portfolio produces a cash flow pattern that is the mirror image of the liability cash flow pattern, everything perfectly offsets.

# Immunity to Interest Rate Changes 

If the risk-free rate changes, it has no effect on economic profit

- The change in InvReturn is exactly offset by the change in EVL
- InvReturn $=3 \%$ interest on previous EVL $+\Delta$ EVL due to interest rate shift

Suppose the risk-free drops to $2 \%$ at time 1 :

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{EVL}_{1} & =\frac{57}{1.02}=55.88 \\
\operatorname{InvReturn}_{1} & =0.03 \times 11.98+(55.88-55.34) \\
& =0.36+0.54=0.90 \\
\Delta \mathrm{EVL}_{1} & =55.88-11.98=43.90 \\
\text { Economic Profit }_{1} & =\underbrace{43}_{\text {No Change }}+\underbrace{0.90}_{0.54 \text { higher }}-\underbrace{43.90}_{0.54 \text { higher }}=0
\end{aligned}
$$

The replicating portfolio has a higher InvReturn because rates dropped (i.e. the bonds appreciated), but now the final liability cash flow of 57 is discounted by lower rate as well

If cash flows end up different than expected, economic profit $\neq 0$

Economic profit is designed to capture the impact of insurance experience

# Treasury Function and Transfer Pricing 

A company could set up a treasury function to attribute performance to underwriting (insurance) decisions vs. investments decisions

Total Profit $=$ Insurance Profit + Investments Profit
The treasury function determines the benchmark total return for the investment unit of the company

The treasury function:

1. Borrows assets equal to the replicating portfolio from the underwriting function
2. Loans assets equal to total capital to the investments function at a rate equal to the benchmark return

By specifying the benchmark return, the treasury function effectively determines the base CoC
When actual investment returns are earned on capital, the investments function pays the treasury interest on the loan

If the benchmark return for $\$ 100$ million of capital is $7 \%$, and the actual return is $8 \%$, then the investment function contributed $\$ 1$ million to economic profit

In the end, the goal is to simply isolate economic profit caused by investment experience to the investments function, not the insurance function. The insurance function's investment return on the replicating portfolio should not impact economic profit since the roll-forward of the replicating portfolio should be offset by cash flows-and if it's not, it is because of insurance experience, not investments experience (see previous examples above).

# Impact of Mortality Volatility 

To reflect mortality volatility, the authors developed a stochastic mortality model based on 3 aspects of mortality risk:

1. Trend risk - uncertainty in future mortality improvement

- Mortality improvement does not follow smooth/predictable trends
- Affected by both long-term waves and random annual fluctuations
- 3 factors affecting trend risk:

1.) Long-term trends (10-years)

- Driven by changes in medical practice/research, society, economy, politics, and the environment

2.) Annual volatility

- Drivers: Extreme weather, disease, and variations in reporting

3.) Correlation between long-term and annual trend volatility
4. Basis risk - uncertainty in the assumed mortality level

- Reflects the risk that a given insurers' mortality experience isn't $100 \%$ of industry experience ${ }^{44}$
- Was modeled independently for the term and SPIA products since sales/underwriting approaches are likely different for those products
- Basis risk is lower for insurers with higher certainty in their best estimate assumption

3. Long-term underwriting risk - uncertainty in how long it takes for underwriting effects to wear off (i.e. transition from select to ultimate mortality)

- Modeled by looking at 3 independent variables:
(a) Uncertainty around initial select period length
(b) Length of grading off period for preferred or substandard mortality
(c) Ultimate mortality level after underwriting has completely worn off

Traditional economics has turned out to be a poor predictor of consumer decision making

- Traditional economics assumes people are "rational agents" (make rational decisions that maximize utility)
- People should choose investments with that maximize probability-weighted utility
- Example: $50 \%$ chance of winning 500 is better than a $10 \%$ chance of winning 1000
- People are assumed to be risk averse

# Main Postulates of Prospect Theory 

Prospect theory says that people's attitude toward risk is determined jointly by the following:

## 1. Reference dependence

- People make decisions about gains and losses relative to a reference point (usually the status quo)
- People are short-sighted ("narrow-framed") - focus more on short-term deviations in utility/wealth than long-term


## 2. Loss aversion

- Losses hurt more than gains of the same magnitude
- Evolutionary explanation: not gambling with what you possess increases your chances of survival


## 3. Diminishing sensitivity to gains and losses

- People prefer small, sure gains over large, unlikely gains
- People are more risk averse following a gain (in the "gain domain")
- Concave utility: additional gains provide less and less additional utility
- People are more likely to seek risk following a loss (in the "loss domain")
- Convex utility: Additional gains provide increasing additional utility


## 4. Probability weighting

- People are bad at evaluating tail events
- Tend to overweight low probability events and underweight high probability events

General interpretation of prospect theory: buying insurance is a loss domain decision

- Individuals prefer to be uninsured (assume risk) because they overweight the certain loss of paying premiums and/or underweight the low probability insured event
- Premiums $>$ E[Loss on death]


## Cognitive Biases

This list consolidates the overlapping "key cognitive biases" listed in the main body of the reading with the "select cognitive biases" table on p. 16 of the reading

## 1. Procrastination and status quo bias

- People tend to delay making unpleasant, demanding decisions
- Buying insurance presents many opportunities to procrastinate, especially if medical underwriting is involved
- People tend to like things to stay the same (status quo)

## Page 137
# 2. Time-inconsistent preferences 

- People make short-sighted decisions they later regret
- Hyperbolic discounting - people prefer immediate payoffs over future payoffs
- Tendency is higher the closer the future payoff is to the present
- "Would you prefer a $\$ 1$ today or $\$ 3$ tomorrow?" - many people choose $\$ 1$
- One study found that people MUCH prefer a lump sum payment to a stream of future annuity payments
- The implied discount rate was $20 \%$


## 3. Framing

- Decisions are affected by how information is presented and who presents it
- Annuity purchases are greatly affected by framing
- Default options - going with a default option is often easier than making a decision
- Plays on status quo bias
- If consumers had to opt out of insurance instead of opt in, sales would likely increase


## 4. Overconfidence and risk perception

- People often overestimate their abilities and overestimate favorable outcomes (don't think they need insurance)
- $93 \%$ of drivers think they are better drivers than the median
- Risk of dying at a young age is usually ignored
- Once the probability of an event is below a certain level, it is assumed to be zero

5. Availability bias and salience - Perceived risk increases after certain events occur (becomes salient)

- More likely to buy property insurance after an earthquake or life insurance after a $9 / 11$ event


## 6. Information overload

- When presented with too much information and choices, consumers either fail to act or make poor decisions
- Evidence shows people are less likely to buy a good or service if there are too many options


## 7. Heuristics (mental short cuts) and herding

- People would rather avoid complex decision making and just follow their peers' choices or social norms
- People are more likely to buy life insurance if friends/family do

## Page 138
- Complexity also arises from regulation and technical terminology
- Heuristics - simplified experience-based techniques for decision making (rules of thumb, "common sense," etc.)


# 8. Mental accounting 

- People implicitly allocate money to different "mental accounts"
- More likely to spend money in certain "buckets" than others

9. Narrow framing

- Type of risk aversion caused by only taking short-term consequences into account without considering total wealth

Key insight from behavioral economics: changing the context in which decisions are made and how information is presented has a big impact on consumer actions

Downside to behavioral economics: many aspects are very context-specific and cannot be generalized

## Trends in US Mortality Improvement

US life expectancy increased at a diminishing rate from the middle of the 20th century through the first decade of the 21st century

In the 2010s, life expectancy plateaued, then fell at the end of the decade
The authors of the article solved for implied mortality scalars that reproduce historical life expectancies and then converted those into MIRs

- Mortality scalar $=$ factor applied to mortality rates in year $t$ that results in the same life expectancy as mortality rates in year $t+1$
- Year-on-year MIR $=1-$ mortality scalar

For example, if 2011 mortality is $99.34 \%$ of 2010 mortality, there was a $0.66 \%$ mortality improvement rate

## Observations in Historical Mortality Improvement

- Mortality has generally improved each decade since the 1940s (MIRs $>0$ )
- Male and female MIRs were different but followed the same general trend
- MIRs have been volatile, but dampened somewhat in the last 30 years
- Mortality deteriorated (increased) in the 2010s, which had multiple years with negative improvement (MIRs $<0$ )
- COVID-19 drove much of this but not all

## Page 147

- Even after removing COVID-19-specific deterioration in 2019-2020, male and female mortality still deteriorated slightly in 2019-2020

It is difficult to predict future MIRs based on past MIRs because mortality trends are influenced by a variety of uncertain factors

## Mortality-Indexed Life Insurance

In the final part of the paper, the authors propose a new type of life insurance product to mitigate the uncertainty of future mortality improvement. They don't really give it a concrete name. They refer to it as both a "mortality-indexed product" and a "life expectancy-indexed product." Either label is fine for exam purposes since graders will know what you're referring to.

One way insurers could reduce their risk of future mortality deterioration is to share the risk with policyholders using a product with premiums indexed to a mortality or life expectancy index

- If population life expectancy falls (rises), premiums would rise (fall)

Could have similarities to par or NGE products, but not the same

- Not a par product since no policyholder dividends involved
- Not an NGE since premiums would be based on an index, not the insurer's expectations
