## Fundamental Concepts

Generally Accepted Accounting Principles (GAAP) are the conventions, rules, and procedures that define accepted US accounting practices

- Stock vs. mutual companies
- To operate, insurers need capital, which is raised from stockholders (for stock companies) and policyholders (for mutual companies)
- 2 primary types of companies owned by stockholders:

## Page 5
1. Publicly traded - issue shares of stock that are traded on exchanges
2. Privately held - not traded on an exchange

- Stockholders expect to earn a return based on the riskiness of their investment
- Policyholders of mutual companies expect coverage at a reasonable price
- GAAP reporting provides transparency to stockholders, policyholders, and related parties
- Financial statements are the primary sources of GAAP information
- Provide summarized information about an enterprise's assets, liabilities, and how transactions affect earnings
- Expressed in units of a single, uniform currency unadjusted for inflation
* Non-monetary information is converted to monetary (e.g. value of equipment)
- Measures a single entity, not the entire industry
* Subsidiaries are consolidated into a single set of financial statements
- Reportable segments are based on operating segments
* Operating segment $=$ relatively homogenous segment of a company as viewed by the company's decision-makers
* May be different company to company
- Information is not intended to be the sole determinant of company value
- Information includes transactions that have already taken place (e.g. premium payments) and estimates of future amounts using actuarial methods (i.e. reserves)
- Measured over a clearly defined period (quarterly or annually)
* Many users want to see performance over shorter periods-challenging for life insurance, which is very long-term in nature
- Cash vs. accrual accounting
- 2 basic types of transactions impact financial statements: cash events and accruals
- Cash basis accounting is the simplest: record accounting entries only when cash changes hands
* Not appropriate for incomplete or long-term transactions (like life insurance)
- Accrual accounting alters the timing of when cash transactions are recognized
* Involves making accrual adjustments to cash transactions so that revenues and expenses are allocated more evenly over the measurement period
* Reserves and DAC are examples
- General purpose vs. specialized purpose financial reporting

## Double-Entry System

GAAP accounting entries are based on the double-entry system ${ }^{3}$, which involves tracking debits and credits

## Balance Sheet

The balance sheet has 3 main sections ("elements"): assets, liabilities, and equity

- Assets $=$ Liabilities + Equity
- Equity $=$ capital consisting of:
- Paid-in stock equity
- Additional paid-in capital
- Retained earnings

- Provisions for losses may be required if the probable future economic benefit declines
- Can be tangible (financial instruments, property, etc.) or intangible (goodwill, patents, etc.)
- Reinsurance creates a recoverable asset for the ceding company

# Income Statement 

Comprehensive income $=$ net income + other comprehensive income (OCI)

- Comprehensive income $=$ all changes in equity during a period except those resulting from owner investments or distributions
- Net income $=$ Revenues - Expenses
- Revenue $=$ Premiums + Fees + InvInc
- Expenses $=$ Benefits + Expenses
- Expenses include not only cash outflows but also decreases in accounting assets (e.g. DAC) and increases in liabilities (e.g. policy reserves)
- Realized gains and losses are also included in revenue and expenses, respectively
- Other comprehensive income (OCI) = changes in equity arising from gains and losses that "are not a central part of the company's operations"
- "Gains" and "losses" refer to changes in equity other than retained earnings and other than owner contributions/distributions
- Examples of items recorded in OCI: ${ }^{4}$
* Unrealized gains and losses on available-for-sale (AFS) assets
* Shadow adjustments for certain liabilities backed by AFS assets
* Impact of discount rate revisions for traditional long-duration insurance
* Changes in the fair value of market risk benefits (MRBs) attributable to own credit risk

EOY Equity = BOY Equity + Comprehensive Income + Owner Transactions

- Owner Transactions $=$ Capital Contributions - Dividends

# Recognition and Measurement of Financial Statement Elements 

Recognition - recording of an item using the appropriate treatment at the appropriate time
SFAC No. 5 criteria for recognition and measurement:

- Definition: asset, liability, revenue, or expense
- Measurability: must have an attribute that is quantifiable with sufficient reliability

* Historical cost: easy to determine using company records but may need to be adjusted for depreciation
* Fair value: may be obtained from active secondary markets or expert appraisals
* Present value: based on cash flows discounted at a rate that can vary (more subjective and less preferable than the other 2, but required for the vast majority of life insurance reserves)

- Relevance: consistent, comparable, and meaningful in users' decision-making

## Financial Statement Accounts and Inter-Relationships

Accrual adjustments are made to cash transactions so that earnings emerge in proportion to the "completeness" of the earnings process

- Premium-related liabilities defer recognition of premium revenue until premiums are earned
- Unpaid claim liabilities provide for benefits already incurred but not yet settled
- A liability for future policy benefits (LFPB) provides for future claims not yet incurred
- DAC defers acquisition costs so that they are recognized more evenly over the contract term

Short-duration unearned premium reserve (UPR) example:

- The insurer will establish an initial UPR of 75 , where $75=$ unearned portion of the premium collected at BOY
- Q2 UPR $=50$, Q3 UPR $=25$, Q4 UPR $=0$

- Q2 net income $=0$ in premium collected $-(-25)$ increase in UPR $=25$, etc.

Dual entry (aka double entry) accounting is often hard to understand for anyone that hasn't taken accounting courses or studied accounting history. It dates back to the 14th century, and the main idea is to track the flow of money or "economic benefit" from sources (credits) to destinations or uses (debits). In other words, it assumes money cannot be created-it can only be transferred from one place to another. Balancing debits and credits allowed ancient accountants to verify the accuracy of their ledgers.

The key to "getting it" is to force your brain to ignore the usual definition/connotation of the words "debit" and "credit." Specifically, don't view those words as "subtractions" and "additions." Credits include liabilities and revenue, while debits include assets and expenses.

This chapter provides some fairly detailed examples of double-entry accounting involving a range of ILA products. We've included another example and commentary in the video lesson to help explain what's going on, and we will revisit some of the UL and VUL examples later in the course after we've covered important concepts in those accounting models in later chapters. I don't think the double-entry accounting examples for those products will make much sense until you get through Ch. 7.

- Stock and mutual life insurers
- P\&C insurers
- Captive insurers
- Reinsurers
- Fraternal benefit societies

ASC 944 applies only to insurance entities
Insurance contracts issued by non-insurance entities (e.g. a manufacturer's warranty) are accounted for under a different standard

Contracts issued by insurance entities are classified as either short- or long-duration

# Overview of Contract Classification 

Contracts should be assessed individually (not in groups) and only at issue
Contracts should first be assessed to determine if they are an insurance contract
To be classified as an insurance contract, the contract must transfer other-than-nominal insurance risk

- Insurance risk = underwriting risk + timing risk
- Excludes investment risk
- "Fortuitous": insured has no control over occurrence of adverse events
- Underwriting risk: uncertainty in ultimate amount of net contract cash flows
- Timing risk: uncertainty in timing of net contract cash flows

If the contract's insurance risk is not nominal, classify it as an insurance contract

- "Nominal" means insignificant or has only a remote chance of occurring (requires judgment)
- Common examples of insurance risk: death, disability, endowment maturities
- It is possible for a contract to be classified as insurance under GAAP but not other accounting standards (IFRS, statutory, etc.)

## Page 17
If the contract has no insurance risk or only nominal insurance risk, determine whether it is an investment contract, service contract, or something else

# Significance of Insurance Risk in Life and Health Contracts 

Most products sold as "life insurance" or "health insurance" in the US transfer significant insurance risk (no testing required)

- WL, term, and most health premiums are relatively small compared to the benefits payable UL contracts with higher PHABs may require additional analysis:
- Determine a significance ratio

$$
\frac{\text { PV(Excess Payments) }}{\text { PV(Assessments) }}
$$

- Excess payments = benefit payments in excess of the policyholder account balance (PHAB)
- Assessments = amounts assessed to the contract including investment margins
- Investment margin $=($ Net Inv Rate - Credited Rate $) \times$ PHAB

There is no specific threshold for the significance ratio (judgment required), but the higher the ratio, the more significant the mortality/morbidity risk

- I.e. if the ratio is too low, it's not insurance

A UL contract with a very high PHAB relative to the DB would be more likely to fail the significance ratio test

- For example, a single premium UL (SPUL) policy would have a very high initial PHAB, which would generate large investment margins for the life of the contract
- The authors show an example of a SPUL contract with a 4\% significance ratio, which they say is too low (not enough insurance risk to be classified as an insurance contract). There is no specific cutoff, however, so it's ultimately a judgment call.

Most modern UL contracts will have a sufficiently high significance ratio

- Since the 1980s, tax law has required UL DBs to be sufficiently larger than PHABs to receive favorable tax status ${ }^{5}$

The significance ratio framework can be applied to other forms of insurance when there is any doubt

# Significance of Insurance Risk in Annuity Contracts 

Life-contingent immediate (payout) annuities are classified as life insurance
Immediate annuities can pay life-contingent benefits, non-life-contingent benefits, or both (aka "certain and life")

Those that combine life- and non-life-contingent payments are still considered insurance contracts unless:

- Probability of life-contingent payments is remote
- PV of life-contingent payments is insignificant relative to all payments

The investment component does not need to be separated unless it is distinct from the rest of the contract

There is no prescribed approach for assessing the remoteness of life-contingent payments
The authors suggest looking at the following ratio over a range of scenarios:

$$
\frac{\text { PV(Life-Contingent Payments) }}{\text { PV(Total Payments) }}
$$

As long as this ratio exceeds $5 \%$ to $10 \%$, the annuity should be classified as an insurance contract The ratio will be lower for:

- Older issue ages
- Longer certain periods

For example, a 30-year certain and life annuity issued to a 65-year-old would have a very low probability of LC payments (i.e. would be considered an investment contract)

Annuities that pay additional insurance benefits that vary with capital markets volatility may also be classified as insurance contracts

A guaranteed annuitization option on a deferred annuity does not itself result in insurance contract classification

- If/when annuitization occurs, it generates a new contract (an immediate annuity), which can then be evaluated


## Identifying Other Components of Insurance and Investment Contracts

For UL-type and investment contracts, additional accounting models may apply:

## 1. ASC 944 market risk benefits (MRBs)

- Most common example: GMxBs on VAs
- $\mathrm{MRB}=$ contract or contract feature that both provides protection to the policyholder from capital market risk and exposes the insurer to other-than-nominal capital market risk

## Page 19
- Other-than-nominal means that it varies significantly with capital market volatility
- Accounted for at FV and shown separately from other items in financial statements
- Change in FV goes through income except for the portion attributable to a change in the insurer's own credit risk, which is reported in OCI
- Criteria for evaluating whether a contract/feature is a MRB:
- "Protection" means the insurer is exposed to capital market losses when the AV < benefit offered (aka a "shortfall" in account balance)
* This risk has been passed from contractholder to insurer
- DB protection provided by a life insurance contract is not a MRB
- "Nominal risk" - risk amount is insignificant or has a remote probability of occurring
- Much more detail on MRBs in Ch. 11


# 2. ASC 815 embedded derivatives 

- A contract is a derivative if all 3 ASC 815 criteria are met:
(a) Contains an underlying and one or more notional amounts or payment provisions
(b) Does not require an initial net investment to enter the contract
(c) Contract terms permit/require net settlement or allow for delivery of an asset of similar value
- Not in scope of ASC 815:
- Any contract/feature classified as an MRB under ASC 944 (do the MRB assessment first)
- Any contract/feature that pays a benefit based on an insured event (falls under ASC 944)
- Some insurance contracts have embedded derivatives that can be separated from the host contract (e.g. IUL or indexed annuities)
- Account for the embedded derivative under ASC 815 and the host under ASC 944


## ASC 606: Revenue recognition for non-financial goods or services

- Applies to a contract/feature that isn't an insurance or investment contract under ASC 944
- The insurer should first evaluate the entire contract under ASC 944
- If a service provided with an insurance/investment contract could be sold on a standalone basis, it should be bifurcated

## Page 20

- Claims adjudication and processing (for claims above or below a deductible)
- Health insurance activities related to fulfillment of the contract
- Safety inspection performed for P\&C contracts
- Roadside assistance (auto policies)
- Cybersecurity activities (general liability policies)
- Title search (title insurance)

5. Recognize revenue as the entity satisfies a performance obligation

# Assessment of Contract Duration 

## Short-duration contracts:

- Provide insurance protection for a fixed period of short duration (usually $\leq 1$ year)
- Allow the insurer to cancel the contract or adjust provisions (e.g. premiums) at the end of any contract period


## Long-duration contracts:

- Generally not subject to unilateral changes in provisions
- Requires the insurer to perform various functions/services for an extended period

Most P\&C and health contracts are short-duration (e.g. home, auto, warranties)

- True even if coverage/pricing is guaranteed for more than 1 year because of the nature of the relationship

Life insurers rarely write short-duration individual policies

- Examples: certain kinds of ART life contracts, credit life, and disability

- Noncancelable - insurer is not allowed to cancel the policy or increase premiums for any reason
- Guaranteed renewable - insurer cannot unilaterally cancel the policy, but the premium rates may be increased subject to certain conditions (e.g. regulatory approval or adverse experience)
- Re-rating must be by class, not individual contract

# Short-Duration Measurement Model 

Unlike long-duration contract types, there is only one short-duration measurement model under ASC 944

- Recognize premiums as revenue over the coverage period in proportion to the amount of insurance protection provided
- Liability = gross unearned premium reserve where the premium is earned ratably over the premium period

- Establish claim liabilities for ICOS, IBNR, etc. using current estimates of future claims payments

# Traditional and Limited-Payment Contracts 

Key contract characteristics: contract terms are fixed and guaranteed

- Examples: term, WL, and LTCI

- Establish claim liabilities for pending, resisted, and IBNR

- Amortize against insurance inforce (or benefit payments for annuities)

Key contract characteristics: contract terms are NOT fixed and guaranteed

1. Policyholder account balance (most significant)
2. Additional liability for excess benefits or profits followed by losses

3. Sales inducement liabilities and assets (SILs and SIAs)

- Examples of sales inducements: immediate bonuses, persistency bonuses, or enhanced crediting rates

4. Unearned revenue liability (URL)

5. Premium deficiency liability

# Certain Participating Life Insurance Contracts 

Many par WL policies issued by mutual companies and certain stock companies follow the contribution principle and have their own ASC 944 measurement model ${ }^{10}$

Par policies that follow the contribution principle have all of the following characteristics:

- Long-duration contracts expected to pay policyholder dividends based on the insurer's actual experience
- Annual policyholder dividends are paid in a manner that both
- Identifies divisible surplus
- Distributes surplus according to the contribution principle (i.e. distributes surplus in approximately the same proportion as the contracts' contribution to surplus)

The total liability under the model for these policies is the sum of 3 items:

1. NLP reserve for death and endowment benefits

- Similar to the LFPB but using locked-in assumptions and a different discount rate

2. Terminal dividend liability
3. Liability for loss recognition (premium deficiency)
4. Policyholder dividend obligation for closed blocks resulting from demutualizations

## Some Terminology Notes

The authors note that the term "cost" can be used interchangeably with "expense." They generally use the word "cost" in this chapter to refer to any non-benefit outflow from the insurer.

The word "expensed" is also important to understand in a GAAP context. While some costs/expenses are expensed as incurred, others are deferred and expensed in the future through mechanisms like DAC. The word "expensed" basically means "recognized," as in when an expense/cost is recognized in income. For example, while acquisition costs are mostly incurred at issue, they are expensed over the life of the contract by way of DAC amortization.

# Deferrable Acquisition Costs 

Acquisition costs are associated with the sale of insurance and investment contracts
Acquisition costs eligible for deferral are capitalized into a DAC asset, which amortizes over the life of the contracts

- Other names for DAC include unamortized acquisition costs (UAC), deferred policy acquisition costs (DPAC), and unamortized expense assets (UEA)
- DAC is an asset held on the GAAP balance sheet, and changes in GAAP go through GAAP income

Only acquisition costs directly related to the successful acquisition of new/renewal contracts (aka "successful efforts") can be deferred:

1. Incremental direct acquisition costs
2. Any employee compensation not included in (1) that is an incremental direct cost of acquisition:
(a) Underwriting
(b) Policy issuance/processing
(c) Medical and inspection
(d) Salesforce (time/resources spent selling products)
3. Other costs directly related to items under (2) that would not have occurred if the contract wasn't acquired

Determining whether a given expense meets the criteria above often requires judgment

- Different companies may arrive at different judgments, but that's fine as long as each company is consistent in its method and documents it

Any costs/expenses that do not meet the deferrability criteria are expensed as incurred on the income statement

## Examples of Deferrable Acquisition Costs

1. Commissions (most common deferrable expense)

- Includes commissions paid to agents and brokers
- Typically directly related to acquisition and successful sales
- Short-duration contracts: defer entire commission
- Long-duration: defer the excess over ultimate commissions
- Based on the commission rate, not commission in dollars
- Flexible-premium UL: target premium is the most common premium base used
* Less common: use projected ultimate premium

## Page 28
- Special cases:
- LP contracts: defer all premium-based commissions (ultimate amount = zero)
- Contracts where a declining commission rate and increasing premium pattern result in roughly level commissions: no commissions are deferrable
- Agent financing costs - e.g. commissions paid in advance or other types of loans
- Usually not included in DAC
- These are capitalized into a different receivable asset
- Commission vesting schedules usually result in declining commission schedules
- Can result in lower ultimate commission rates
- Fund-based commissions - paid as a \% of fund value on UL and deferred annuities
- Not deferrable if the rate does not directly vary with new business or is not related to a successful acquisition
- Level rate would never be deferrable
- Non-level rate could result in deferrable amounts, but practice varies and care should be taken

# Definition of DAC Amortization Basis 

## Short-Duration Contracts

Amortization follows the premium and coverage period
The general approach is to release DAC ratably over the coverage period in proportion to the recognition of premiums

There is not a specific formula prescribed for this, but the authors illustrate a recommended approach that we also demonstrate numerically in the video lesson, which can be expressed in formula form as:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{DAC}_{t} & =\text { DeferrableCosts } \times \frac{\text { Unearned Premium }}{\text { Total Premium }} \\
\mathrm{DAC} \text { Amort }_{t} & =\mathrm{DAC}_{t-1}-\mathrm{DAC}_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

The main idea is that the DAC balance will always be based on the percentage of remaining unearned premium. Simple example: suppose you capitalize 1000 at the beginning of the year, and premiums are paid annually at BOY. The initial DAC will be 1000, followed by 750 at EOQ 1, 500 at EOQ 2, etc. DAC amortization is simply the change in DAC balance between reporting dates. If premiums change during the year, DAC must be trued up, and we illustrate that in the video lesson as well.

## Investment Contracts

For DAC purposes, investment contracts are contracts with no significant mortality/morbidity risk or surrender charges

- I.e. substantially all revenue comes from investment spreads
- Examples: GICs, period-certain annuities, etc.

DAC amortization follows the "interest method" (aka effective yield method)
For a period-certain single premium immediate annuity:

1. Solve for an $\operatorname{IRR}\left(r_{\text {gross }}\right)$ such that the PV of benefits at issue equals the gross premium received

- $\operatorname{GrossLiab}_{t}=\mathrm{PV}_{t}($ Future Ben $)$ using $r_{\text {gross }}$

2. Solve for second $\operatorname{IRR}\left(r_{\text {net }}\right)$ such that the PV of benefits at issue equals the gross premium received minus deferrable acquisition costs

- NetLiability ${ }_{t}=\mathrm{PV}_{t}($ Ben $)$ using $r_{\text {net }}$

3. Implied $\mathrm{DAC}_{t}=\operatorname{GrossLiab}_{t}-\operatorname{NetLiability}_{t}$

# Long-Duration Contracts 

This approach is applicable for all long-duration insurance contracts (term, WL, UL, etc.) as well as any deferred annuity with surrender charges or any investment contract with significant sources of revenue other than investment margins or asset-based fees

Even though the approach described here is the most important DAC approach to know for the exam since it applies to most products, it is very difficult to understand in words alone. You'll want to rely more on the video lesson's numerical examples to grasp what's happening here.

DAC must be amortized on a constant-level basis over the contract's expected term

- Individual contracts: amortize DAC on a straight-line basis
- If calculating DAC for groups (cohorts) of contracts: approximate straight-line amortization using a basis such as the projected amount of insurance inforce
- Other bases like policy count may be more appropriate if there is no stated face amount (will be covered later in product-specific chapters)

* If DAC is tracked at the contract level, it will be written off if a contract terminates for any reason
* The grouped approach is designed to allocate DAC amortization according to the distribution of future inforce ${ }^{16}$
- Groups should be the same as those used for the LFPB
- There is no requirement to perform any quantitative materiality analysis to justify the approximation

# Recognize actual termination experience immediately in income 

- Individual basis: Write off DAC immediately for terminating contracts (death or lapse)
- Grouped basis: true-up DAC amortization to reflect higher-than-expected terminations in the current period
- No adjustment is made if terminations are less-than-expected

Alternative grouped method: reflect current period experience in the beginning-of-period DAC

- This approach avoids the need to make a true-up adjustment or any additional DAC writedowns since it waits until the current period's experience is known

While either approach is allowed, the first grouped approach generally seems like the default approach in most GAAP literature, including multiple chapters of this book. We demonstrate both approaches in the video lesson.

## Long-Duration DAC Formulas

As with short-duration contracts, there is no specific formula codified anywhere for long-duration DAC, but the following formulas represent the most foolproof framework for any situation, including when assumptions change for future experience:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { AmortRatio }_{t} & =\frac{\left(\text { DAC }_{t-1}+\text { Capitalization }_{t}\right)}{\sum_{t}^{n} \text { BOYIF }_{t}} \\
\text { DACAmort }_{t} & =\text { AmortRatio }_{t} \times \text { BOYIF }_{t} \\
\text { DAC }_{t} & =\text { DAC }_{t-1}+\text { Capitalization }_{t}-\text { DACAmort }_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

where

[^0]
[^0]:    ${ }^{18}$ This term coined by the authors is unique to this book and is easier to illustrate through example in the video lesson.

- Capitalization $_{t}=$ deferrable expenses incurred and capitalized in year $t$
- $\mathrm{BOYIF}_{t}=\mathrm{BOY}$ inforce for year $t$ (can be expressed as face amount inforce, units inforce, etc.)
- AmortRatio $_{t}=$ current DAC plus any new capitalizations as a level percentage of the sum of future inforce
- This causes DAC amortization to emerge as a level $\%$ of BOY inforce

If terminations are higher-than-expected (which would cause the actual BOY inforce to be lower-than-expected), calculate a true-up ${ }^{19}$ (aka experience adjustment) to DAC amortization:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{TrueUp}_{t} & =\frac{\text { OrigBOYIF }_{t}-\text { ActualBOYIF }_{t}}{\text { OrigBOYIF }_{t}} \times \text { OrigDAC }_{t} \\
\mathrm{DAC}_{t} & =\mathrm{DAC}_{t-1}+\text { Capitalization }_{t}-\mathrm{DAC} \text { Amort }_{t}-\text { TrueUp }_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

The true-up adjustment reflects the additional DAC written off for terminations in the period This is a one-time adjustment and does not have any effect on the amortization ratio unless the insurer also changes assumptions for future inforce

# Unit of Account 

ASC 944 provides little guidance on the level of aggregation for the liability for future policy benefits (LFPB)

While seriatim reserves are technically possible, it would make it difficult to unlock the net premium ratio (NPR), which is required by ASC 944

- If reserves are seriatim and a contract terminates, there is no way to reflect that in the NPR since the contract is no longer inforce
- Therefore, some level of grouping is necessary

ASC 944 states that contracts within a group must be in the same cohort (issued within 1 year of each other)

- Commonly, this means each group is composed of contracts from the same issue year

# Liability for Policy Benefits (LFPB) 

Terminology note: the LFPB is ASC 944's formal name for the entire GAAP reserve. It technically includes all reserve components, including claims reserves. The reserve for future benefits is often informally called the "benefit reserve." For traditional non-par business, the benefit reserve is by far the most significant component of the LFPB since claims reserves tend to be small by comparison. As such, we will generally use "LFPB" to refer to the reserve for future benefits/claims that have not yet been incurred.

The LFPB for traditional long-duration contracts (term, WL) is based on a net level premium approach, where reserve premiums $=$ constant $\%$ of gross premium

$$
\mathrm{LFPB}_{t}=\mathrm{PVFB}_{t}-\mathrm{NPR}_{t} \times \mathrm{PVGP}_{t}
$$

Net premium ratio (NPR) $=\%$ of actual and expected GPs required to provide for all benefits and claims-related expenses over the lifetime of the policy ${ }^{25}$

$$
\mathrm{NPR}_{t}=\frac{\mathrm{PV}_{0}(\text { Actual Benefits Through } t)+\mathrm{PV}_{0}(\text { Expected Benefits After } t)}{\mathrm{PV}_{0}(\text { Actual GPs Through } t)+\mathrm{PV}_{0}(\text { Expected GPs After } t)}
$$

The LFPB can never be negative at the group level chosen (i.e. floored at zero)
The NPR is capped at $100 \%$

- This means that there are no separate premium deficiency reserves required for traditional long-duration contracts ${ }^{26}$
- Recoverability testing for the value of business acquired (VOBA) or PV of future profits (PVP) is still required ${ }^{27}$

At least once per year, the NPR must be recalculated as of the issue date (i.e. retrospectively from $t=0$ ) after replacing expected benefits with actual benefits up through the valuation date

- Always use at-inception discount rates (called the interest accretion rates) for all PVs in the NPR calculation
- Estimates of claims-related expenses (LAE) like termination costs, settlement costs, etc. do not have to be updated if the insurer chose to lock them in ${ }^{28}$
- Whenever the NPR is updated to reflect actual cash flows, the actuary must also revise or reconfirm all cash flow assumptions

    ${ }^{26}$ Capping the NPR at $100 \%$ basically means you are not allowed to assume more future premium than the GP actually paid by the policyholder.

## Remeasurement Gain or Loss

When the NPR is updated with revised assumptions, it causes the LFPB to be different from what it would have been otherwise

This difference is recorded explicitly as a remeasurement gain or loss
Calculate beginning-of-period (BOP) (e.g. beginning of year, quarter, etc.)

- If done quarterly, the annual remeasurement gain/loss = sum of quarterly remeasurement gains/losses

Remeasurement Loss (Gain) = BOP LFPB Using Updated NPR - BOP LFPB Using Previous NPR

- If the updated NPR causes the BOP LFPB to be lower, this creates a gain; otherwise it creates a loss

The total benefit expense on the income statement can be broken down as follows:

$$
\begin{array}{ll}
\text { Actual Benefits Incurred } \\
\quad+\text { Remeasurement Loss } & \text { (= Revised BOP LFPB - Original BOP LFPB) } \\
\text { + Change in Reserve } & \text { (= EOY LFPB - Revised BOP LFPB) } \\
\text { = Total Benefit Expense } &
\end{array}
$$

Where the "revised" LFPBs are based on the revised NPR (combination of updating for actual experience and/or revised future assumptions)

- The intermediate step of calculating the remeasurement loss essentially breaks the total reported reserve change into two pieces

The revised NPR and remeasurement gain/loss are always based on the original locked-in discount rate(s)

# Discount Rate Revisions 

Discount rates should be updated at each reporting date (e.g. quarterly) to reflect current the upper-medium grade yields

The impact of updating discount rates is recorded in other comprehensive income (OCI)

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{OCI}_{t} & =\mathrm{AOCI}_{t}-\mathrm{AOCI}_{t-1} \\
\mathrm{AOCI}_{t} & =\text { accumulated OCI at time } t \\
& =\mathrm{LFPB}_{t} \text { based on original } \mathrm{DR}-\mathrm{LFPB}_{t} \text { based on current } \mathrm{DR}
\end{aligned}
$$

Therefore, insurers will have to continue calculating the policy liability using the at-inception discount rate so they can calculate the difference above

The NPR and all previously discussed reserve calculations at each reporting date are always based on the original discount rate(s) at inception

## Page 44
- The increase in reserve is based on the locked-in discount rate is called "interest accretion"

The video lesson includes a numerical illustration of this concept.
The main idea is to ensure that the "normal" part of the income statement is always based on the same DR from inception. Even though I've never seen GAAP literature refer to it this way, you can basically think of the LFPB valued at current discount rates as the "fair value" of the liability. This makes the liability more consistent with AFS assets backing the liability-both are effectively carried at FV on the GAAP balance sheet. OCI reflects the change in the current liability based solely on changing the DR assumption period to period and helps "connect" the income statement to the balance sheet, so to speak. ## Approaches for the Locked-In Interest Accretion Rate

The ASU does not specify how to calculate the locked-in interest rate used for interest accretion, but several approaches could be used

1. Spot rate approach - apply the spot rate to each year-specific cash flow

- Always use the original spot rate for each cash flow through time
- This method requires tracking the liability through time using the original term structure of the cash flows

2. Forward rate approach - apply implied annual (year-specific) forward rates to the total liability balance each year

- Calculate annual forward rates implied by the spot curve first
- As the liability moves through time, use the appropriate forward rate for each year using the original term structure developed at issue
- This method does not require keeping up with the original cash flow term structure since the forward rates vary by year

3. Effective yield approach - apply a constant IRR to the total liability balance all years

- Calculate an implied IRR based on the spot curve and cash flows at inception

[^0]
[^0]:    ${ }^{31}$ When interest rates rise, discount rates will rise, which will lower the liability (create positive OCI). Rising interest rates also lower the market value of assets, which would result in negative OCI.

## Page 45
- This method also does not require keeping up with the original cash flow term structure and simply uses a constant rate

If premiums are payable over multiple years and the yield curve has a normal upward slope, the effective yield may exceed long-term spot rates

- Results from the "leveraging effect" of premiums exceeding benefits in early years then reversing in the future ${ }^{32}$
- Can cause spot rate accretion to be higher than under the effective yield method
- Spot rate-base interest accretion may be a better match with investment income

The forward rate approach usually produces the lowest accretion and lowest reserves in early years

- However, lower early accretion means that accretion will be higher in later years (may exceed investment income)
- Forward curves tend to have steep slopes and sometimes dips/spikes, which will affect interest accretion

# DPL for Limited-Pay (LP) Contracts 

LP contracts have premium payment periods are that shorter than the coverage period

- Single-pay, 10-pay, 20-pay, payments to age 65, etc.

LP contracts follow the same accounting model for traditional long-duration contracts but also require a deferred profit liability (DPL) when the NPR $<100 \%$

- DPL is based on gross premium received in excess of net premium used in the benefit liability
- Amortize the DPL against the discounted insurance inforce (or benefit payments for lifecontingent payout annuities ${ }^{33}$ )

Without a DPL, LP contracts would generate high profits during the premium-payment period, followed by negative earnings after premiums stop (not desirable under GAAP)

## DPL calculation steps:

1. Determine the amount of annual profit to defer

$$
\text { Profit }_{t}=(1-\mathrm{NPR}) \times \mathrm{GP}_{t}
$$

${ }^{32}$ Premiums will typically be higher than DBs early in the life of the policies because the insurer has to charge enough premium to not only cover current DBs but also build a reserve for future DBs.
${ }^{33}$ Life-contingent payout annuities are treated much like traditional long-duration contracts under GAAP and since they involve a single premium upfront, they are also LP contracts. These will be covered in detail in Ch. 12, including their DPL mechanics.

## Page 46
Profit is a BOY value since it's based on premiums (equals zero after the premium payment period ends) ${ }^{34}$
2. Calculate DPL the as a constant $\%$ of inforce

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{DPL} \% & =\frac{\mathrm{PV}_{0}(\text { Profit })}{\mathrm{PV}_{0}\left(\mathrm{BOYIF}^{*}\right)} \\
\mathrm{DPL}_{t} & =\mathrm{DPL} \% \times \mathrm{PV}_{t}\left(\mathrm{BOYIF}^{*}\right)-\mathrm{PV}_{t}(\text { Profit }) \\
& =\left(\mathrm{DPL}_{t-1}+\text { Profit }_{t}\right) \times(1+i)-\mathrm{DPL} \% \times \mathrm{BOYIF}_{t-1}^{*}
\end{aligned}
$$

The BOYIF term is exactly the same amortization basis we used for DAC in Ch. 4, but unlike DAC, we are discounting it with interest in the DPL framework. In the GAAP book authors' DPL example, they treat the BOYIF as though it occurs EOY for interest discounting. ${ }^{35}$ This treatment of the inforce is different from all other examples in the book, but we recommend being consistent with the authors' approach for exam purposes, and we're denoting it BOYIF* as a reminder. See the video lesson for more comments.
3. The change in DPL each period is included in the benefits section of the income statement

- As profit is being capitalized, the DPL rises to offset and defer it
- The DPL declines each period after premiums end releasing profit into income

# Restricted Participating Life 

This section is relevant to stock companies who offer dividend-paying policies that do not follow the contribution principle and who also restrict dividends payable to shareholders. "Restricted dividends" refers to the restricted shareholder dividends.

In some cases, state/federal law, company charter, or the policy form itself will restrict dividends payable to stockholders to protect policyholders' share of profits, which will be distributed through future policyholder dividends

- The policyholder's share of profit is excluded from stockholder equity and credited to par policyholders' funds

If there are no restricted dividends, then any policyholder dividends paid by a non-contribution principle are included as another benefit in the LFPB

If there are restricted dividends, the insurer must establish a separate liability for the dividends called an undistributed participating policyholders' earnings account (UPPEA)

- Purpose: defers profits that will eventually be returned to policyholders through dividends so that the profits are not reported in current income
- Do not include the dividends in the LFPB since they'll be accounted for in the UPPEA

The book shows a simple example where stockholder dividends are restricted to be no more than $10 \%$ of stat profits before policyholder dividends. The formulas below are implied by the example. If the restriction was something other than $10 \%$, you can just change that factor. Note that even if the restriction is based on stat income, you should still use GAAP income in all the UPPEA calculations because UPPEA is specific to GAAP.

## Page 53
1. Project the GAAP income statement (premiums, investment income, benefits, costs, DAC amortization, etc.)
2. Calculate GAAP net income (revenues minus expenses) ignoring policyholder dividends
3. Calculate a running total of GAAP surplus before policyholder dividends

$$
\text { CumSurplus }_{t}=\text { CumSurplus }_{t-1}+\text { NetIncomeBefore }_{t}
$$

4. Calculate a running total of policyholder dividends paid
5. $\mathrm{UPPEA}_{t}=0.90 \times$ CumSurplus $_{t}-$ CumPHDivs $_{t}$

- I.e. set aside a liability for the policyholders' share of cumulative profit ( $90 \%$ ) in excess of the profit that has already been distributed to policyholders through past dividends

6. Final GAAP net income:

$$
\text { NetIncome }_{t}=\text { NetIncomeBefore }_{t}-\text { PHDivs }_{t}-\Delta \text { UPPEA }_{t}
$$

"UL-type" contracts are a specific type of long-duration insurance contract under ASC 944
ASC 944 defines UL-type contracts by distinguishing them from other typical long-duration contracts:

# UL PHAB Monthly Processing 

The authors present a basic formula for computing the monthly PHAB that is very similar in concept to the UL account value formulas we saw in SVILAC Ch. 14. We've reorganized the formulas from this chapter into the following implied steps:

1. Credit premiums net of loads at the beginning of each month (BOM):

$$
\text { PHAB after prem and } \exp =\mathrm{PHAB}_{t-1}+\operatorname{Prem}_{t}\left(1-\text { PremLoad } \%_{t}\right)-\operatorname{ExpLoad}_{t}
$$

2. Calculate COI charge, which is also deducted at BOM:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{COI}_{t} & =\mathrm{COIRate}_{t} \times \mathrm{NAR}_{t} \\
\mathrm{NAR}_{t} & =\frac{\mathrm{DB}_{t}}{\left(1+i_{g}\right)^{(1 / 12)}}-\underbrace{\left(\text { PHAB }_{t-1}+\text { Prem }_{t}\left(1-\text { PremLoad } \%_{t}\right)-\text { ExpLoad }_{t}\right)}_{\text {PHAB after prem and exp }} \\
i_{g} & =\text { guaranteed annual credited rate } \\
\text { PHAB after COI } & =\text { PHAB }_{t-1}+\text { Prem }_{t}\left(1-\text { PremLoad } \%_{t}\right)-\text { ExpLoad }_{t}-\mathrm{COI}_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

The liability described here is another element of the account balance defined by ASC 944. This information comes from Ch. 4, which we put off covering until this point in the course. All of the material here is relevant to not only UL but also deferred annuities, and we will see more examples in Ch. 11.

Ch. 4 uses the phrase "sales inducement liability," which is also sometimes called "the persistency bonus liability." However it's calculated (or however it's labeled) the liability is part of the account balance liability reported on the GAAP balance sheet.

ASC 944 defines sales inducements as amounts that:

- Are explicitly identified in a contract
- Paid in excess of current market conditions


## 3 main types of sales inducements:

1. Immediate bonus (day-one bonus)
2. Persistency bonus
3. Enhanced-crediting-rate bonus (paid in first year or first few years only)

SIs paid in the first year do not require an additional liability since they are already part of the PHAB

- However, the insurer may establish a sales inducement asset to defer recognition (covered later)

## Page 67
Persistency bonuses are a common example of something that gives rise to an "additional interest" liability

- These pay the policyholder an amount in the future if the contract is still inforce

Common examples of persistency bonuses:

- One-time bonus
- Higher credited rate
- Refund of some or all COI or expense charges
- Some other discontinuous increase to the PHAB

Since a persistency bonus isn't paid until a future date, ASC 944 requires the insurer to accrue a liability that is added to the PHAB when determining the LFPB

ASC 944 requires the liability to accrue ratably with no allowance for surrender decrements Possible methods:

1. Straight-line over the persistency period
2. As a level percentage of the account balance
3. As a level percentage of assessments (COI charges, investment spreads, and expense charges)

SILs are added to the account balance component of the LFPB
SIL accruals (changes) are included in benefit payments on the income statement
For example, assuming a constant credited rate $r$ and a persistency bonus will be paid at the end of the $n$th year: ${ }^{53}$

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { AccrualFactor } & =\frac{\mathrm{PB}_{n} /(1+r)^{n}}{\bar{a}_{\bar{n}}} \\
\text { BonusLiab }_{t} & =\left(\text { BonusLiab }_{t-1}+\text { AccrualFactor }\right)(1+r)-\mathrm{PB}_{t} \\
& =\frac{\mathrm{PB}}{(1+r)^{(n-t)}}-\text { AccrualFactor } \times \bar{a}_{\bar{n}-t}
\end{aligned}
$$

The accrual factor is a level BOY dollar amount that accumulates with interest to the persistency bonus amount

As the bonus liability increases each year, it reduces GAAP income (essentially like laying on an extra piece of credited interest)

At time $n$ when the persistency bonus is paid, the bonus liability will be exactly equal to that amount and will be released to offset it

The net effect is to spread the expensing of the persistency bonus over the $n$-year period (so it doesn't hit income all at once at time $n$ )

Changes in a SIL for a persistency bonus can also be capitalized as incurred into an SIA
ASC 944 allows a SIA to be established if the SI is explicitly defined in the contract at inception and if it meets the following criteria:

1. Incremental to amounts the company credits on similar contracts that do not have the SI
2. Expected to be higher than the contract's ongoing crediting rates for periods after the inducement

SIAs are computed using the same amortization basis and method used for DAC

$$
\begin{aligned}
\text { AmortRatio }_{t} & =\frac{\left(\text { SIA }_{t-1}+\text { Capitalization }_{t}\right)}{\sum_{t}^{n} \text { BOYIF }_{t}} \\
\text { Amort }_{t} & =\text { AmortRatio }_{t} \times \text { BOYIF }_{t} \\
\text { SIA }_{t} & =\text { SIA }_{t-1}+\text { Capitalization }_{t}-\text { Amort }_{t}
\end{aligned}
$$

The process and formulas are literally the same as DAC, but just remember that a sales inducement is not a deferrable acquisition cost. For example, bonus interest credited in year one is not an acquisition cost, so it would never be included in DAC. However, by including it in an SIA, the same goal is accomplished: smoother recognition of expenses and earnings. The SIA is also shown in the asset section of the balance sheet (don't net it with SILs).

# Fixed Deferred Annuities (FDAs)

# Variable Annuities (VAs) 

VAs allow policyholders to allocate funds to a fixed account and one or more investment subaccounts backed by separate account assets

## Defining characteristics of VAs:

- Investment options - may include a variety of equity, fixed income, and balanced fund options as well as a fixed fund
- Fees - includes M\&E charges and investment management fees
- Death and living benefits (GMxBs) - may offer GMDBs, GMIBs, GMABs, and GMWB/GLWBs based on:

- Roll-up: GMxB = deposits less withdrawals, accumulated at a guaranteed interest rate
- Ratchet: GMxB resets at certain dates (e.g. anniversary) if PHAB > previous GMxB
- High-water mark: GMxB = max PHAB over a specified period of time
- Reset: GMABs may reset the starting minimum guaranteed value and begin a new guarantee period at certain dates


## Fixed Indexed Annuities (FIAs)

FIAs allow the policyholder to allocate money to an indexed account, which can rise with an index but protected from downside risk

FIAs are general account products and are subject to nonforfeiture law like regular FDAs
Option budget $=$ cost incurred by the insurer to fund index credits

- Usually based on expected investment income less a pricing spread
- Analogous to a credited interest rate on an FDA


## Defining characteristics of FIAs:

- Index used - S\&P 500 500, etc.
- Index crediting period - credits could be applied annually or based on index performance over a period of years
- Usually starts when premiums are deposited, but could be based on month-end values to simplify administration
- Flexible premium designs "bucket" premiums and track new index period for each
- Participation rate - percentage of index growth applied as an index credit (e.g. 70\%)
- Cap rate - max index credit allowed by the contract

## Page 74
- Floor rate - lowest index credit allowed by the contract ( $0 \%$ is common)
- Index spread - deducted directly from the index return before determining index credit (reduces index credit) ${ }^{56}$
- Guaranteed minimum rate - the lowest rate that can be credited over the life of the contract Simple example of an index credit formula: ${ }^{57}$

$$
\text { Credit }=\max \left[\text { FloorRate, } \min \left[\text { Cap, PartRate } \times \text { IndexReturn }- \text { IndexSpread] }\right]\right.
$$

Some FIAs follow a binary design:

- If the index return exceeds a specific \%, it credits a guaranteed rate
- Else, the credit is zero or something between zero and the index return


# Possible index credit approaches: 

- Point-to-point (PTP) - based on index performance between two points in time (1 or more years apart)
- Annual reset (1-year PTP) - index credits are applied at the end of each policy year
- Participation rate, cap, floor, etc. may reset for the next contract year
- Later, the authors refer to this design as a "ratchet-type" design
- Monthly average - based on the average index performance over a period of time
- High-water - based on the max index value over a period of time relative to the starting index

FIAs may offer GMxBs similar to VAs, but with less risk for the insurer since FIA account balances have much less downside potential

## Registered Index-Linked Annuities (RILAs)

RILAs are like FIAs where the policyholder shares downside risk with the insurer

- Like a hybrid between VAs and FIAs from the policyholder's perspective
- Key difference with a FIA: a RILA policyholder's PHAB may decrease

The policyholder's downside risk is limited in one of two ways (inverse of each other):

1. Floor: The policyholder bears losses up to a floor, after which the insurer bears all losses

$$
\text { Credit }=\max \left[\text { FloorRate, } \min \left[\text { Cap, PartRate } \times \text { IndexReturn] }\right]\right.
$$

This formula is identical to an FIA (excluding the spread for simplicity). The only difference is that the RILA floor is likely to be set lower than $0 \%$ (e.g. $-5 \%$ ).

## Benefit Reserve for Indexed Annuities

As with IUL, the reserve for FIAs and RILAs is not simply the PHAB, but rather a host value plus the value of an embedded derivative (VED)

- ASC 815 classifies index credits as an embedded derivative that must be bifurcated and fair valued separately from the host
- ASC 815 expressly excludes index credits from the definition of an MRB ${ }^{59}$
- At issue, the host liability $=$ net premium collected - initial VED
- The initial VED is called the "host offset"
- At issue, initial host value + VED exactly equals the net premium (deposit) paid into the contract after deducting any FELs, etc. (no gain or loss at issue)
- After issue, the host liability grows at a break-even rate (IRR)
- The IRR is a solved-for rate at issue that grows the initial host value to the guaranteed maturity value of the contract
- The VED is always measured at fair value at each valuation date
- The host is treated as a debt-like instrument

# Option Budget Method 

The option budget method is a simplified single-scenario method commonly used in practice instead of doing a more complex stochastic valuation

- Based on the idea that the option budget (as a $\%$ of the index account balance) is a good estimate of expected annual index credits
- Usually based on product pricing
- Option budget $=$ expected yield on fixed income assets - pricing spread
- Fixed income yield is based on the company's target asset mix
- Some insurers assume a single option budget rate for all years; others vary it
- Contract cash flows are projected using the option budget rate and assumptions for mortality, lapse, and withdrawal
- VED $=\mathrm{PV}$ of excess benefits in this projection relative to the projected guaranteed PHAB
- Care should be taken to ensure the method follows ASC 815 requirements for fair valuation:
- Maximize the use of market-observable information
* How: use observable risk-free rates on valuation date
- Include a provision for nonperformance risk (own credit risk)

* How: add an adjustment to risk-free rates (increase them)
- Include a provision for uncertainty in cash flows
* How: adjust actuarial cash flows and/or decrease discount rates


# Alternatives to the Option Budget Method 

- Treat index credits as a partial net settlement of the embedded derivative
- VED $=$ PV of expected future index credits, not expected future CFs

- More sophisticated stochastic approaches
- VED = average PV of excess index benefits over stochastic interest rate and equity scenarios
- Index credits vary by scenario based on assumed parameters
- Some insurers are beginning to use this in place of the OBM as computing/modeling techniques improve


## Host Contract Valuation

- Current host value $=$ PV of future guaranteed CFs at the current IRR

## Market Risk Benefits (MRBs)

MRB = contract or contract feature that both provides protection to the policyholder from capital market risk and exposes the insurer to other-than-nominal capital market risk

- Most common example: GMxBs on VAs
- "Other-than-nominal" means that it varies significantly with capital market volatility
- Accounted for at FV and shown separately from other items in financial statements
- Change in FV goes through income except for the portion attributable to a change in the insurer's own credit risk, which is reported in OCI
- Criteria for evaluating whether a contract/feature is an MRB:
- "Protection" means the insurer is exposed to capital market losses when the AV < benefit offered (aka a "shortfall" in account balance)
- This risk has been passed from contractholder to insurer
- "Nominal risk" - risk amount is insignificant or has a remote probability of occurring


## Features that are not MRBs:

- DB protection provided by a life insurance contract
- Guaranteed minimum crediting rate on FDA


## Situations requiring judgment to determine if a feature is an MRB:

- Guaranteed annuity purchase rates that contain both capital market and mortality risk
- It may be possible to assess capital market risk if mortality and interest rates in the guaranteed purchase rates are defined
- A GMDB on a VA where the PHAB is so low that there is very little capital market risk remaining (more of a pure insurance contract)
- The probability of death is irrelevant-only the exposure to capital market risk matters

## Page 81
If any plausible scenario exists where the insurer would be exposed to significant capital markets risk, it would be "more than remote" (classify as an MRB)

# Valuation of MRBs 

## MRBs are measured at fair value at the individual contract level

- Aggregation is not allowed unless the resulting liability is not materially different than contract-level calculations

ASC 944 states that MRBs can be measured using either an option or non-option approach

- Under either approach, the initial gain or loss should be zero (at issue only)
- If a contract has multiple MRBs, they must be bundled as a single compound MRB

In practice, these option and non-option approaches have converged into a single, unified approach:

1. Identify the explicit fees charged to the PHAB

- Includes rider charges, M\&E, and asset-based charges
- Excludes investment margins (credited interest spreads) and surrender charges

2. Project all excess benefits and contract fees under a range of stochastic scenarios using market-consistent (risk neutral) assumptions
3. Calculate the PV of excess benefits and contract fees in each scenario

- Discount rate $=$ scenario risk-free rate + instrument-specific credit risk ${ }^{63}$

4. Average the PV of excess benefits and contract fees across all scenarios
5. Calculate the portion of the explicit fees attributable to the MRB: attributed fee percentage (AF\%), capped at $100 \%$ :

$$
\mathrm{AF} \%=\min \left(100 \%, \frac{\text { Avg PV of Excess Benefits }}{\text { Avg PV of Explicit Fees }}\right)
$$

$\mathrm{AF} \%$ is locked-in at issue and reused on future valuation dates (step 7)
As long as $\mathrm{AF} \%<100 \%$, there is no gain/loss at issue (value of MRB $=\mathrm{PV}$ attributed fees at issue)

- The cap is more likely to apply to FIAs that rely on investment margins to fund the MRB (since investment margins are excluded from the explicit fees)

6. If the $\mathrm{AF} \%$ is capped, lock it in at $100 \%$ and establish an "adjustment" liability to the value

# A Note about DAC for Investment Contracts 

As we saw in Ch. 3, ASC 944 defines any contract without significant mortality/morbidity risk as an investment contract (as long as it's not an ASC 606 services contract), and as noted in Ch. 4, investment contracts use the effective yield method for DAC. Most fixed and variable deferred annuities without guarantees that lead to benefits in excess of the PHAB will be technically classified as investment contracts.

However, the convention under US GAAP has long been to use the same DAC method for these contracts as other long-duration products as long as the contracts have surrender charges. ${ }^{69}$ Since virtually all deferred annuities have surrender charges, a deferred annuity classified as an investment contract will use the same DAC method we've used for all other long-duration contracts (even if it's beyond the SC period). This point is not made clear anywhere in the current edition of the GAAP book, but Ch. 4 does subtly mention the fact that an investment contract without SCs would use the effective yield method.

The current edition of the GAAP book does not demonstrate the effective yield method for a deferred annuity in any of the chapters included on the syllabus. In practice a deferred annuity without any surrender charges would be rare, so it probably wasn't a priority. In Ch. 12, we will see how to apply the effective yield method to payout annuities without significant mortality risk. That same approach can be used for a deferred annuity without surrender charges.

## Definition of a Market Risk Benefit

MRB = a benefit in addition to the account balance that protects a policyholder from other-thannominal capital market risk and exposes the insurance company to other-than-nominal capital market risk

The assessment of other-than-nominal capital market risk occurs at contract inception

- A guarantee could be an MRB in some economic environments but not others
- "Nominal" means "remote probability of occurring"
- "Other-than-nominal" also means "the benefit would vary by more than an insignificant amount in response to capital market volatility"
- Must consider a range of possible capital market scenarios (not just the current situation)
- Utilization assumptions should not be used in the assessment of other than nominal risk ${ }^{70}$


## Methodology Considerations

MRBs should follow FV accounting
Multiple MRBs on a single contract should be bundled into a compound MRB
MRB FV can be calculated using an option or non-option approach, which is more common
The non-option approach solves for an attributed fee such that the MRB value is zero at inception: PV(MRBs) - PV(Attributed Fees)

Inputs needed for MRB valuation:

- Risk-neutral interest and equity scenarios
- Own credit risk

[^0]
[^0]:    ${ }^{70}$ This is because an MRB that is perhaps very out of the money might not have a high probability of utilization currently but could in the future.

## Page 88
- Policyholder behavior assumptions (lapse, withdrawal, and benefit utilization)
- Risk margins for unobservable inputs/assumptions

Many capital market assumptions are observable and do not require a risk margin

# Process for calculating the non-option MRB liability ${ }^{71}$ 

1. Project all excess benefits and contract fees across each RN scenario
2. Calculate the PV of excess benefits and contract fees in each scenario

- Discount rate $=$ RN interest rate + instrument-specific credit risk

3. Average the PV of excess benefits and contract fees across all scenarios
4. For newly issued policies, calculate the attributed fee percentage (AF\%)

$$
A F \%=\min \left(100 \%, \frac{\text { Avg PV of Excess Benefits }}{\text { Avg PV of Associated Fees }}\right)
$$

This will be zero gain/loss at issue unless $\mathrm{AF} \%$ would exceed the $100 \%$ cap, in which case there would be a loss at issue
5. Calculate the MRB liability at future valuation dates using the locked-in $\mathrm{AF} \%$

MRB Liability $=$ Avg PV of Excess Benefits - Avg PV of Attributed Fees

Much of the operational challenge will be related to the retrospective transition adjustment required for MRBs

- Will require work to rebuild models from the past so that calculations can be performed from the contracts' original issue dates
- Problems will likely arise from changes in software versions, data availability, lack of documentation, etc.

## Overview of This Reading

This chapter covers GAAP accounting for payout annuities, which differ from deferred annuities in that payout annuities make benefit payments to the annuitant (contractholder)

The measurement model for a payout annuity depends on whether it's an investment contract or an insurance contract

If it's an insurance contract, it follows the same guidance as traditional non-par long-duration insurance (Ch. 5), but there are some important implementation differences

If it's an investment contract, the measurement model is closer to a deferred annuity approach (UL-type), but there are also important implementation differences-namely for DAC

## Key topics for the exam include:

- Sources of and types of payout annuities
- Contract classification: insurance vs. investment
- Investment contract measurement: effective yield approach
- DPL measurement for life-contingent contracts
- Acquisition costs and DAC considerations unique to payout annuities
- Profit emergence


## Overview of Payout Annuities

Payout annuities provide periodic payments to the owner (annuitant)

## Sources of payout annuities:

- Direct sales
- Annuitization of a life insurance death benefit or deferred annuity PHAB
- Structured settlements from legal decisions
- Lottery prizes payable in installments
- GMIBs or GMWBs exercised on a VA
- Retirement income payments from defined benefit pension plans
- Income benefits from contingent deferred annuities


## Forms of payout annuities:

- Payment guarantee: can be life-contingent, term-certain, or both

## Page 90
- Can be fixed or variable
- First payment date: immediate or deferred
- Immediate payout annuities begin payments shortly after contraction inception
- Deferred payout annuities delay the first payment for at least 1 year


# Contract Classification 

Most payout annuities are classified as long-duration since they are expected to be inforce for an extended period

When a payout annuity arises from the proceeds of an existing life or annuity contract, establish a new contract for GAAP accounting purposes

As with other contracts subject to ASC 944, payout annuities must be assessed for mortality risk
Classify payout annuities as investment contracts if they do not have significant mortality risk, which means one of the following are true:

1. Probability of life-contingent payments is remote
2. PV of expected life-contingent payments relative to the PV of all expected payments under the contract is insignificant

If neither is true, classify as an insurance contract
Assessing the likelihood of life-contingent payments requires judgment

- Common threshold: significant means LC payments are expected to be more than 5-10\% of all payments

$$
\frac{\text { PV(LC Payments) }}{\text { PV(All Payments) }}
$$

Example of obvious investment contracts: term-certain payout annuities and structured settlements with large guaranteed lump sums

The classification of certain-and-life payout annuities can vary based on the issue age and termcertain period

- A 10-year certain-and-life payout annuity issued to a 65-year-old would be an insurance contract
- The same contract issued to a 100-year-old would be an investment contract (low probability of surviving the certain period)

Many payout annuities are classified as limited-pay contracts as long as they meet all of the following criteria:

1. Provide a specified benefit payment in exchange for a fixed premium

- Else, classify as UL-type (common for variable payout annuities)

2. Premium payment period < coverage period

## Page 91
3. Does not pay dividends

- Else, classify as participating life insurance

Virtually all fixed payout annuities will satisfy No. 2 since payout annuities are predominately single-premium products

In practical terms, a life-contingent fixed payout annuity is simply another type of insurance contract under ASC 944 and follows much of the same guidance we first saw in Ch. 5. The only differences arise due to implementation details (e.g. how to amortize the DPL.)

# Investment Contract Measurement 

Any payout annuity classified as an investment contract follows the usual rules for investment contract accounting (don't record premiums as revenue, etc.)

The net GAAP liability and DAC are determined using the ASC 310 constant yield method (aka effective yield method)

1. Solve for an constant yield (IRR), $r_{\text {gross }}$ such that:

$$
\mathrm{PVFB}_{0}=\text { Premium }
$$

2. Solve for another constant yield, $\left(r_{\text {net }}\right)$, such that:

$$
\mathrm{PVFB}_{0}=\text { Premium }-\mathrm{DAE}
$$

where $\mathrm{DAE}=$ deferrable acquisition expenses at issue
At each valuation date, calculate the LFPB and implied DAC balance:

- $\mathrm{LFPB}_{t}=\mathrm{PVFB}_{t}$ using $r_{\text {gross }}$
- Net GAAP Liability ${ }_{t}=\mathrm{PVFB}_{t}$ using $r_{\text {net }}$
- $\mathrm{DAC}_{t}=\mathrm{LFPB}_{t}-\mathrm{Net}$ GAAP Liability $_{t}$

Note that $r_{\text {gross }}<r_{\text {net }}$ because LFPB $>$ net GAAP liability
The framework above provides a way of calculating DAC on an "implied" basis. This DAC method is unique to payout annuities classified as investment contracts. See the video lesson for an example.

Remember that a payout annuity classified as an insurance contract follows the same DAC approach as any other long-duration life insurance product, even though there are unique considerations for the amortization basis (described later).

## Limited Pay Insurance Contracts

Life-contingent payout annuities follow the same guidance applicable as any other type of limited-pay insurance contract

The following aspects are unique to payout annuities:

- Aggregation and cohort grouping considerations
- As always, cohorts should contain contracts from the same issue year
- Individual sales and life insurance settlements are put in quarterly or annual cohorts
- Payouts from institutional channels (e.g. pension risk transfer) may be put in singlecontract cohorts (but not always) ${ }^{72}$

- Liability for future policy benefits (LFPB)
- LC payout annuities follow exactly the same guidance as traditional LD life insurance (Ch. 5), but since there is only one premium, the formula simplifies to:

$$
\begin{aligned}
& \mathrm{LFPB}_{t}=\mathrm{PVFB}_{t}-\mathrm{NPR}_{t} \times \mathrm{PVGP}_{t} \\
& \mathrm{LFPB}_{t}=\mathrm{PVFB}_{t}
\end{aligned}

* But simpler since there's usually no NPR to true-up

* With a single premium product, there is technically just one net premium (at time 0 ) equal to the PVFB

# Acquisition Cost Treatment 

Identification of deferrable acquisition costs follows the same guidance as other contracts that fall under ASC 944 (Ch. 4)

The following considerations are unique to life-contingent payout annuities:

- When a payout annuity arises from any source, a new contract begins with its own DAC
- Deferrable expenses are based on deferrable costs incurred to establish the payout
- Payouts resulting from annuitization elections under other contracts give rise to little, if any, DAC
- Payouts sold directly by the insurer are more likely to have a DAC asset
- Expected term for DAC = expected future lifetime of annuitant
- Requires updating each valuation date as expected life changes due to assumptions
- For contracts sold on a group basis (institutional purchases), common practice is to amortize DAC over the individual annuitants' expected life, not the expected term of the group contract (i.e. unit of account $=$ individual contract)
- Possible amortization bases for group DAC:

## Page 95
- Policies inforce: simplest, but usually inappropriate since it doesn't reflect contract size
* Appropriate if contracts are homogenous in size, age, and mortality characteristics
- Initial premium: better choice than policy count because it reflects contract size (amount of expense deferred)
* Disadvantage for institutional contracts: requires allocation of group premium to individual contracts
- Initial or "normal" benefit amount: similar advantages as initial premium
* Disadvantage for institutional contracts: benefit amount may change over time on deferred pension plans
- Current benefit amount: addresses disadvantage of using initial benefit amount, but doesn't meet the definition of "constant-level" basis, so probably not appropriate

The authors of the chapter do not make a firm recommendation on any of the above choices, but it would seem that using initial premium or initial benefit amount is the best choice for typical individual payout annuities-and probably also group contracts even though group contracts require more work to allocate the premium or benefit amount to individual contracts, which are the true unit of account. # Considerations for Variable Payout Annuities 

The benefit payment for a variable payout annuity adjusts over time to reflect the performance of the underlying separate account assets

At issue, the annuitant chooses an expected long-term investment rate from a narrow range (e.g. $3 \%$ to $5 \%)$

- The initial payment is based on this rate, then the payment adjusts over time

Some designs allow the insurer to add/subtract assets based on mortality experience
Most variable payout annuities are classified as UL-type since the benefits are not fixed and guaranteed

- As with a UL contract, the LFPB $=$ PHAB
- Challenge: there is no explicit PHAB, so an implied PHAB must be determined
- Possible method: PHAB $=\mathrm{PV}$ expected benefits at the LT rate elected by the annuitant

Due to the variable nature of the benefit payments, the current benefit payment should not be used to amortize DAC
